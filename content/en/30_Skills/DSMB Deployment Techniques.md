# DSMB Deployment Techniques

In open water, a Delayed Surface Marker Buoy (DSMB, commonly referred to as a "marker buoy" or "sausage") is your primary tool for communicating with the surface: letting the boat captain know your location, marking your ascent point, and allowing searchers to locate you during a drift. **Deploying a DSMB cleanly while maintaining neutral buoyancy** is a skill every sidemount diver must internalize before embarking on boat or drift dives [1][2].

---

## 🎯 Why Sidemount Divers Need Specialized Practice

*   **More Chest Webbing and Hoses**: Sidemount divers carry two second-stage hoses, inflator hoses, and SPG lines across the chest. The path of the spool line has more potential entanglement points than in a backmount configuration.
*   **Deep Deployment for Deco**: Technical sidemount dives often involve decompression obligations. The DSMB should be deployed **before leaving the bottom (rather than during the safety stop)** so the surface boat crew can track your position early (see [[en/60_Advanced/Decompression Stop Practice & Oxygen Deco|Decompression Stop Practice & Oxygen Deco]]).
*   **Neutral Buoyancy is the Standard**: Launching the DSMB must not cause your depth to vary by more than **±0.5 meters**—a skill directly tied to your hovering proficiency (see [[en/30_Skills/In-Water Buoyancy & Horizontal Trim|In-Water Buoyancy & Horizontal Trim]]).

## 🧰 Gear Selection Guidelines

| Item | Recommendation | Reason |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Buoy Body** | 1.4–1.8 m tall, open-ended or sealed with an overpressure relief valve (OPV). | High enough to be seen in swells; OPV prevents the buoy from bursting as gas expands on ascent [2]. |
| **Spool** | **Spool preferred over a mechanical Reel**, 30–45 m line. | No ratchets to jam, compact, fits easily in a utility pocket—inherited from the cave diving philosophy of simplicity [1]. |
| **Connection** | Keep the spool and DSMB detached or connected via quick-release bolt snaps in storage. | For stowage details, see [[en/20_Equipment/Accessories and Pocket Configuration|Accessories and Pocket Configuration]]. |
| **Color Coding** | Orange/Red = normal ascent; **Yellow = emergency** (standard team protocol). | Pre-dive briefs must align on color meanings; do not assume standard protocols across different teams [2]. |

## 📋 Standard Deployment Procedure (Neutral Buoyancy Version)

```
1. Hover ──> 2. Deploy/Unfurl Buoy ──> 3. Verify Spool Connection ──> 4. Inflate ──> 5. Release ──> 6. Wind & Ascend
```

1.  **Establish a Hover & Inform Your Buddy**: Signal "DSMB launch" to your buddy, who will monitor the surroundings (and listen for boat propellers overhead).
2.  **Unfurl the Buoy**: Pull the DSMB from its stowage, unfurl it, and point the opening downward. **Ensure the spool line does not cross any hoses, harness webbing, or body parts**—the line must run straight from the buoy to the spool in your hand, entirely in front of your body profile.
3.  **Inflation Methods** (choose based on buoy design):
    *   **Exhaled Gas Inflation**: Tilt your head slightly to allow exhaled gas from your second stage to bubble into the open end of the buoy—this keeps your regulator in your mouth and is the safest, most widely taught method [1].
    *   **Backup Regulator / LPI Cinch**: Best for sealed buoys with inflator nipples; be cautious of freezing risk when pressing the purge button on a backup regulator.
4.  **Release**: Inflate the buoy to only about 1/3 to 1/2 full (it will expand to full capacity as it rises). **Let the spool spin freely in your hands**—keep your fingers loose on the spool center, never grip it tight.
5.  **Surface Confirmation**: The line going slack indicates the buoy has reached the surface. Pull back gently to tension the line, keep it vertical to maximize visibility, and begin your ascent (see [[en/30_Skills/Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards|Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards]]).

## ⚠️ Safety Boundaries

1.  **Never clip the spool to yourself during deployment**: If the spool jams or the buoy is caught by a boat propeller or surface swimmer, it will drag you rapidly to the surface, causing decompression sickness [1][2]. Once at your stop, if you need to free your hands, lock the spool with a double-ended bolt snap and hold it in your hand rather than clipping it to a harness D-ring.
2.  **Verify your line path before inflating**: A typical DSMB accident involves the spool line wrapping around gear (valves, fin buckles, long hoses) and dragging the diver upward. For entanglement procedures, see [[en/50_Safety/Line Entanglement & Cutting Tool Configuration|Line Entanglement & Cutting Tool Configuration]].
3.  **Deploy your own DSMB**: In drift diving, if you become separated from the team and cannot deploy your own buoy, searchers will not be able to find you (see [[en/50_Safety/Team Separation & Lost Diver SOP|Team Separation & Lost Diver SOP]]) [2].
4.  **Practice in shallow water first**: Practice in water shallower than 10 meters, where the consequences of an uncontrolled ascent are minimized. Progress to deep water and cold-water glove setups as your skills improve.

## 🏋 Practice Progression (Each phase requires depth control within ±0.5m)

1.  Platform knee-hover deployment (focus on muscle memory)
2.  Neutral mid-water hover deployment (core validation)
3.  Blue-water deployment with no visual references
4.  Single-handed deployment (holding a primary light in one hand while managing the spool)
5.  Thick gloves / low-visibility deployment (see [[en/50_Safety/Hypothermia Prevention & Management|Hypothermia Prevention & Management]])
