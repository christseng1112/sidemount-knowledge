# Single Cylinder Sidemount Configuration (Single Sidemount Configuration & Balancing)

Although sidemount diving is primarily designed as a dual-cylinder redundant system, **single cylinder sidemount** is a highly practical configuration for recreational holiday diving, travel destinations where extra cylinders are limited, or lightweight shallow diving. However, the primary challenge of a single-cylinder configuration is the **highly asymmetrical weight and buoyancy distribution**.

This article covers the physical challenges of single-cylinder diving, counter-weighting strategies, regulator and BCD hose routing, and in-water trim compensation techniques.

---

## ⚖ Physical Challenges of Single-Cylinder Sidemount

In a dual-cylinder configuration, weights are balanced on both sides, allowing the diver to maintain lateral stability. When diving a single cylinder:

```
    【Dual Sidemount (Balanced)】                【Single Sidemount (Asymmetric Torque)】
       ▲ Center of Buoyancy (CoB)                 ▲ Center of Buoyancy (CoB)
       │                                          │
 [Tank A]─●─[Tank B]                       [No Tank] ─●─ [Left Tank (~18kg dry)]
       │                                          │
       ▼ Center of Gravity (CoG)                  ▼ Center of Gravity (CoG) ──> Creates left rolling torque
```

1.  **Rolling Torque**:
    *   Using a left-slung cylinder as an example, a full AL80 aluminum cylinder (empty tank ~14.3 kg + compressed air ~2.7 kg + valve and regulator ~1.5 kg) has a dry weight of approximately **18 kg**, whereas its underwater net weight is only -0.7 kg.
    *   However, the dry weight offset and the water resistance create a rolling torque, causing the diver to **roll toward the side of the cylinder (left side)** while swimming [1][2].
2.  **Unilateral Late-Dive Float**:
    *   As gas is consumed and pressure drops below 100 bar, the left-side aluminum cylinder becomes positively buoyant (up to +1.9 kg empty).
    *   This pulls the left side of the body upward, disrupting horizontal Trim.

---

## 🛠 Counter-Weighting Strategies

To counteract the rolling torque of a single cylinder, the most effective solution is to **place counter-weights on the opposite side of the body (the cylinder-free side)** [1][5].

*   **Setup Method** (cylinder mounted on the **left**):
    *   Mount a trim weight pocket on the **right shoulder strap or right side of the waist belt**, adding **1.5 to 2.5 kg (3-5 lbs)** of lead ballast [1][5][6].
*   **Calculating Ballast Weight**:
    *   Rather than calculating for the extremes of a full or empty cylinder, balance for the **midpoint of the dive (approx. 100 bar, where an aluminum cylinder is near-neutral in the water, but the first stage and valve still weigh approx. 1.5 kg)**.
    *   Placing 1.5 to 2.0 kg of ballast on the right side balances the lateral rolling torque, making swimming comfortable throughout the dive [1][5][7].

---

## 📐 Regulator & BCD Hose Routing

A single cylinder is typically mounted on the **left side**. This allows the primary regulator hose to exit from the left, wrap around the neck, and enter the mouth from the right, maintaining the standard long hose routing [3][4].

*   **Regulator Setup**:
    *   **Primary Regulator**: Uses a **1.0 to 1.5 m (40-60 inch)** hose with a 90-degree or 120-degree elbow adapter, routed from the left cylinder behind the neck to the mouth. Alternatively, a standard 2.1m long hose can be used, with the excess length clipped to the right waist belt D-ring [3][4][7].
    *   **Backup Regulator**: Mounted on a bungee necklace around the neck.
*   **Low-Pressure Inflator (LPI) Routing**:
    *   Exits from the left regulator **5th port (axial port)** to connect to the BCD inflator. **Hose length is determined by the BCD inflator valve position**: if the 5th port runs straight up the cylinder to a nearby valve, a **15 cm (6-inch)** hose is sufficient; routing requiring more length uses a **30–38 cm (12–15 inch)** hose, which is the mainstream sidemount LPI range [3][9].
*   **Managing the Empty Side**:
    *   Pull the right-side neck bungee tight and stow it flat against the shoulder harness or clip it to the waist D-ring to prevent it from dangling and causing drag.

---

## 🏊 In-Water Balancing & Swimming Adjustments

If your harness cannot accommodate a counter-weight pocket, use the following body mechanics to maintain trim [5][8]:

1.  **Core Muscle Compensation**:
    *   If the cylinder is on the left without counter-weights, **contract your right obliques and core muscles** to pull the right side down, preventing a left-side roll. Because this can cause muscle fatigue after a long dive, it is not recommended for extended periods [5][8].
2.  **Fin Canting**:
    *   While swimming, tilt the right fin slightly downward (like a ship's rudder), using the hydrodynamics of the kick to push the right side of the body back up to horizontal [8].
3.  **Early Sliding D-ring Relocation**:
    *   In a single-cylinder configuration, the upward lift of a positive aluminum tank bottom is highly noticeable because there is no counter-weight on the right. **Slide the Sliding D-ring forward earlier than in dual-cylinder configurations** (at approx. 120 bar) to prevent the floating cylinder from pulling your harness out of alignment [8][10].
