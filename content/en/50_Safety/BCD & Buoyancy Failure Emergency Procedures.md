# BCD & Buoyancy Failure Emergency Procedures (Buoyancy Control Failures)

Underwater buoyancy control failures (such as a stuck BCD inflator valve causing a runaway ascent, or a bladder rupture leading to a complete loss of buoyancy) are high-risk emergencies. A runaway ascent can lead to severe Decompression Sickness (DCS) or pulmonary barotrauma, while a total loss of buoyancy can cause a diver to sink uncontrollably.

This article details how to manage runaway inflation and implement backup buoyancy options (dual bladders, drysuit inflation, and ballast weight ditching) to provide a complete emergency response guide.

---

## 🚀 1. Stuck BCD Inflator Valve (Runaway Inflation)

When a BCD inflator valve button jams in the "open" position due to sand intrusion, freezing, or mechanical failure, high-pressure gas streams into the bladder, dragging the diver toward the surface [1][2].

### 🚨 Emergency SOP
Divers must react within **3 seconds** to prevent an uncontrolled ascent:

```
【Runaway Inflation Response】
 1. Dump Air ───────────> 2. Disconnect LPI ────────> 3. Shutdown Valve (Emergency)
    Pull dump valve with    Squeeze and pull LPI     If hose is jammed, close right
    one hand to vent gas.   collar to release hose.  valve to stop gas, pull collar [2].
```

1.  **Step 1: Dump Air**:
    *   Do not look for the hose first! Immediately pull your BCD's lower dump valve cord or raise your inflator hose assembly over your head and press the manual exhaust button. Vent incoming gas continuously to maintain stable buoyancy [1][2].
2.  **Step 2: Disconnect the LPI Hose**:
    *   While venting gas, use your other hand to grasp the collar of the low-pressure inflator (LPI) quick-disconnect coupling. Pull the collar back and slide the hose off the inflator nipple. Once disconnected, gas flow stops [1][2].
3.  **Step 3: Emergency Valve Shutdown**:
    *   If you are wearing thick gloves or diving in cold water, the coupling may be frozen or jammed. In this case, immediately turn your right cylinder valve **clockwise to close it** (since BCD inflator hoses are typically supplied by the right first stage). Once the valve is closed, pressure in the LPI line drops, stopping the inflation. Disconnect the hose, and then re-open the right valve to restore gas flow to your primary regulator [2][3].

## 🛑 2. BCD Bladder Rupture or Catastrophic Leak

If your BCD bladder is torn by rusty metal or a dump valve O-ring dislodges, the bladder will fail to hold air, and you will lose neutral buoyancy. You must implement a backup buoyancy option [2][4]:

### 🟢 Option A: Deploy a Dual-Bladder BCD (Dual Bladder)
*   **Verify your gear specifications first**: Many sidemount wings (including the **standard XDEEP Stealth 2.0 / 2.0 Tec**) feature a "double-layer Cordura shell and nylon bladder" design, which represents a **single inflation chamber**, not redundant dual bladders [9]. In contrast, the **Razor 4 BAT Wing** comes standard with redundant dual bladders (each providing 45 lbs / 20 kg of lift) [10]. Check your gear configuration before diving.
*   **If you are using a dual-bladder system**:
    1.  Stop using the primary inflator valve and locate the **backup inflator assembly** (typically secured to the left harness shoulder strap).
    2.  Inflate the backup chamber (the backup LPI hose is supplied by the left regulator first stage, maintaining source redundancy).
    3.  Use the backup bladder to restore neutral buoyancy and abort the dive [4][5].

### 🔵 Option B: Utilize a Drysuit (Drysuit Inflation)
*   **Drysuit as Backup Buoyancy**: If you are diving in a drysuit, the suit can serve as backup buoyancy [5][6].
    1.  If the BCD bladder fails, inject gas directly into the drysuit chest valve.
    2.  Be aware that drysuit lift is limited by internal gas volume, suit squeeze, and the exhaust valve position. **Actual lift capacity in a drysuit is much lower than a BCD wing** and should not be treated as a direct equivalent.
    3.  During your ascent, vent gas through the shoulder exhaust valve to control your ascent rate.
*   ⚠️ **Redundancy debate**: Using a drysuit as the sole backup buoyancy option is only acceptable when **the diver's rig is not heavily negative** (e.g., using aluminum cylinders or carrying ditchable ballast). **If diving heavy steel cylinders or carrying multiple stages, a drysuit may not provide enough lift at depth**. Agencies like GUE require redundant buoyancy matched to the overall negative weight of the gear configuration; evaluate your rig's negative buoyancy profile [5][6][7].

### 🟡 Option C: Ditch Ballast Weight (Drop Weight) — Last Resort
*   If you are diving in a wet suit with a single-bladder BCD and suffer a catastrophic bladder rupture, you have no backup inflation chambers.
    1.  To prevent sinking, **ditch a portion of your ballast weights** [2][6].
    2.  *Ditching rule*: Do not drop all your weights at once. Open your weight pockets and drop lead blocks **one by one** until you achieve neutral or slightly positive buoyancy.
    3.  Maintain a horizontal Trim, kick with your legs, and swim diagonally toward the surface to abort the dive [6][8].

## 🏊 3. Swimming Up Without Buoyancy: Maintain Horizontal Trim

When swimming upward to overcome gravity without BCD buoyancy, a common mistake is to **stand upright in the water and bicycle-kick toward the surface** [7][8].

*   **Drag Penalty**: Standing upright increases your frontal surface area, boosting drag and reducing kicking efficiency. It also prevents you from looking ahead at your ascent line and monitor your buddy.
*   **Correct Posture**: **Maintain a flat horizontal Trim**. Look forward along your ascent path and engage your core. Use frog kicks or modified frog kicks to convert your thrust into forward-and-upward vector force (swimming at a 15 to 30-degree angle). This minimizes drag and conserves gas during the ascent [7][8].
