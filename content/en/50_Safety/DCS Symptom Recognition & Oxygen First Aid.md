# DCS Symptom Recognition & Oxygen First Aid (DCI Recognition & Oxygen First Aid)

> ⚠️ **This article serves as a first aid review and does not replace PADI/DAN Emergency Oxygen Provider courses or equivalent hands-on training.** If you suspect decompression illness, contact DAN or a diving medicine specialist immediately—**even if symptoms appear to have subsided** [1].

---

## 🫧 Terminology: DCI = DCS + AGE

**Decompression Illness (DCI)** is an umbrella term covering two distinct medical conditions that share similar field first aid protocols [1]:

| Parameter | Decompression Sickness (DCS) | Arterial Gas Embolism (AGE) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mechanism** | Rapid ascent or insufficient decompression; dissolved nitrogen bubbles out in tissues and bloodstream [1]. | Breath-holding during ascent $\rightarrow$ alveolar rupture $\rightarrow$ gas enters arterial circulation [1]. |
| **Typical Onset** | Within 15 minutes to 12 hours after surfacing [1]. | Within **minutes** of surfacing; extremely rapid onset. |
| **Severity** | Ranges from mild (joint pain) to severe (spinal paralysis). | **Immediate life threat** [1]. |

---

## 🚨 Symptom Identification

### Common DCS Presentation [1][2]
*   **Deep, throbbing joint pain** (knee, ankle, elbow, shoulder are most common—"the bends").
*   Numbness, tingling, or muscle weakness.
*   **Extreme, disproportionate fatigue** (the most frequently overlooked early sign).
*   Skin mottling (cutis marmorata) or itching (skin bends).
*   Vertigo, dizziness, and hearing loss.
*   Chest pain and coughing when breathing (the "chokes"—pulmonary DCS, severe).
*   Loss of coordination (ataxia), difficulty urinating, or paralysis (spinal DCS, severe).

### AGE Presentation [1]
*   Dizziness, blurred vision, convulsions, unilateral paralysis/weakness, loss of consciousness, and respiratory arrest.

> 📌 **Field Rule: Treat any unusual neurological symptoms or unexplained joint pain after a dive as DCI**. Denying symptoms ("let's wait and see") is the leading cause of delayed treatment.

---

## 🧯 Field First Aid SOP [1][2]

1.  **Immediate 100% Oxygen**: Emergency oxygen is the single most effective field intervention—it speeds up nitrogen elimination and oxygenates compromised tissues [1][2]. Use a demand valve regulator or a non-rebreather mask set at 15 L/min. **Administer oxygen continuously until the supply is exhausted or medical help arrives; do not stop if symptoms improve**.
2.  **Positioning**: Keep conscious casualties lying flat (supine). If unconscious, place them in the **recovery position** (on their side) in case of vomiting [1]. The Trendelenburg position (head down, feet up) is no longer recommended.
3.  **Hydration**: Offer water to conscious, alert casualties who can swallow safely. (DCS is heavily aggravated by dehydration) [1][2].
4.  **CPR**: Initiate CPR immediately if the casualty stops breathing and has no pulse [1].
5.  **Contact DAN Emergency Hotline +1-919-684-9111 (24-Hour)** or local emergency services to coordinate transport to a **recompression chamber** (hyperbaric oxygen therapy is the only definitive treatment) [1][2].
6.  **Document Dive Profile**: Pass the casualty's dive computer to emergency responders and document a timeline of symptom onset.

### ❌ Contraindications
*   **Never attempt in-water recompression** (taking the diver back down to depth to "squeeze" bubbles). Unless conducted by a highly trained team with specialized equipment, this carries a high risk of worsening the condition [2].
*   Do not administer alcohol or caffeine, and avoid hot showers (vasodilation can accelerate bubble growth).

## 🛡 Prevention & Preparation

*   **Prevention**: Control your ascent rate and perform safety stops (see [[en/50_Safety/Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards|Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards]]), configure conservative Gradient Factors (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Decompression Planning Software & Gradient Factors|Decompression Planning Software & Gradient Factors]]), and monitor your CNS/OTU exposure (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Oxygen Exposure & Nitrogen Narcosis Management|Oxygen Exposure & Nitrogen Narcosis Management]]).
*   **Sidemount Readiness**: Technical sidemount decompression diving increases DCI risk profiles. An emergency oxygen kit should be a standard component of your team's shore or boat support gear [2].
