# Ear Equalization & Barotrauma (Equalization & Ear Barotrauma)

Middle ear barotrauma has the **highest incidence rate** of all diving-related injuries. For sidemount divers, there is a frequently overlooked constraint: **in tight overhead environments, the standard solution of "ascending a bit to try equalizing again" may not be an option**—your head is limited by a rock ceiling. Equalization must be a second-nature, zero-failure skill before entering restricted spaces [1][2].

---

## 👂 Decompression Physiology of the Ear

The middle ear is a closed air cavity linked to the throat via the **Eustachian tube**. During descent, ambient water pressure increases. If you do not actively push gas into the middle ear, the eardrum is pressed inward—causing fullness, pain, and eventually injury. **The most rapid rate of pressure change occurs in the shallowest zone between 3 and 10 meters**, which is the most critical area for equalization [1].

---

## 🛠 Six Equalization Methods [1]

| Method | Execution | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Valsalva Maneuver** | Pinch nose, blow gently | Most common; **over-exertion carries inner ear risks**. Keep it "gentle, short, and frequent." |
| **Toynbee Maneuver** | Pinch nose, swallow | Gentle and low-risk; ideal for mid-descent adjustments. |
| **Frenzel Maneuver** | Pinch nose, push back of tongue up while making a "K" sound | Highly efficient; does not increase chest pressure. Recommended as the primary technical diving method. |
| **Lowry Technique** | Pinch nose, blow and swallow simultaneously | Combination of Valsalva and Toynbee. |
| **Voluntary Tubal Opening (VTO)** | Tense throat muscles, push jaw forward as if yawning | Hands-free—**the ultimate technique when hands are busy managing spools and lights**. |
| **Passive Equalization** | Occurs naturally during ascent | No actions required. |

**Frequency**: Equalize "early, often, and gently"—equalize **before** you feel pressure, approximately **every 0.6 meters (2 feet)**. Equalizing after pain presents is too late [1][2].

---

## 🧗 Managing Equalization Failures

1.  **Stop descending**—never attempt to push through ear pain.
2.  **Ascend 1–2 meters**, relax, swallow several times, and try another equalization method [2].
3.  If ears remain blocked $\rightarrow$ **terminate the dive**. A single dive is not worth a ruptured eardrum.
4.  **Overhead Constraint**: Verify that both ears equalize easily in open water before entering an overhead environment. Test your ears before entering vertical cracks or shafts where overhead clearance is limited (see [[en/60_Advanced/Sidemount Guidelines & Operations in Overhead Environments|Sidemount Guidelines & Operations in Overhead Environments]]).

## 🔄 Reverse Block (Reverse Squeeze)

A reverse block occurs when expanding gas in the middle ear or sinuses fails to escape during ascent, causing sharp pain or vertigo. **This is almost always caused by congestion from a cold or from a nasal decongestant wearing off during the dive** [2].

*   **Management**: Stop ascending $\rightarrow$ descend 0.5–1 meter to relieve pressure $\rightarrow$ ascend slowly in segments to allow the gas to find an exit. **Do not attempt a Valsalva maneuver** (adding gas to the middle ear will worsen the block) [2].
*   **Gas Reserve Implications**: Resolving a reverse block requires gas (Time × Depth × RMV). Your Rock Bottom gas reserve must cover this slow, forced ascent profile (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles|Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles]]).
*   **Prevention**: If you have a cold or congestion, do not dive. Relying on pseudoephedrine or spray decongestants to equalize is dangerous; if the drug wears off at depth, a reverse block presents in a location where you have no abort options.

## ⚠️ Injury Identification & Medical Ceilings

| Symptoms | Possible Injury | Action |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Post-dive fullness, hearing loss, fluid sensation | Middle ear barotrauma (effusion/hemotympanum) | Cease diving; seek medical review if symptoms persist for several days. |
| Sudden sharp pain followed by relief, vertigo, or tinnitus | Eardrum perforation | Abort dive; seek immediate medical care. No diving until eardrum is fully healed. |
| **Vertigo + tinnitus + noticeable hearing loss** | **Inner ear barotrauma or Inner Ear DCS** | **Medical Emergency**. Inner Ear DCS requires recompression, whereas inner ear barotrauma is a contraindication for recompression. Must be evaluated by a hyperbaric specialist (see [[en/50_Safety/DCS Symptom Recognition & Oxygen First Aid|DCS Symptom Recognition & Oxygen First Aid]] and [[en/70_Resources/Diving Insurance & Emergency Evacuation Planning|Diving Insurance & Emergency Evacuation Planning]]). |

If you experience sudden unilateral vertigo underwater (caloric/pressure vertigo): grasp a reference object or your buddy, stop depth changes, and wait for it to pass—it typically resolves within a few dozen seconds. Vertigo in mid-water without visual references can be disorienting (refer to search orientation in [[en/50_Safety/Team Separation & Lost Diver SOP|Team Separation & Lost Diver SOP]]).
