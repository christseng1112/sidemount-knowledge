# Controlled Emergency Swimming Ascent (CESA) & Last Resort Options (CESA & Last-Resort Options)

CESA (Controlled Emergency Swimming Ascent) is a core open-water skill: the procedure for **swimming to the surface on your own** when you are completely out of gas and have no access to alternative gas sources [1][2]. For sidemount divers, CESA occupies a unique niche—**independent dual cylinders make CESA almost obsolete, but "almost" does not mean "never."**

---

## 📉 Sidemount Gas Contingency Priority List

When gas delivery is interrupted, attempt the following options in descending order:

1.  **Switch to the other cylinder** (a core sidemount advantage: takes 2 seconds; see [[en/30_Skills/Second Stage Stowage, Switching & Retrieval|Second Stage Stowage, Switching & Retrieval]]).
2.  **Close the valve on the leaking cylinder** to isolate the failure (see [[en/50_Safety/Sidemount Regulator Malfunction & Leak Management|Sidemount Regulator Malfunction & Leak Management]] and [[en/50_Safety/Valve Drills Standard Operating Procedures|Valve Drills Standard Operating Procedures]]).
3.  **Share gas from a buddy's long hose** (see [[en/50_Safety/Out of Gas (OOG) & Long Hose Sharing Procedure|Out of Gas (OOG) & Long Hose Sharing Procedure]]).
4.  **CESA** (This article: shallow depth, no buddy, both cylinders disabled).
5.  **Buoyant Emergency Ascent**: Ditching all weights to float rapidly to the surface (the last-resort option when depth is shallow but CESA is not possible; carries the highest risk of pulmonary over-expansion injury) [2].

A scenario where both sidemount cylinders are disabled is extremely rare (e.g., contaminated gas in both tanks or simultaneous freezing). However, options 4 and 5 exist to provide a final fallback in the emergency decision tree.

---

## 🏊 Standard CESA Procedure [1][2]

**Applicable Depth**: Typically trained at 6–9 meters. The shallower the depth, the higher the success rate. If depth exceeds ~18 meters, success rates drop, and options 1–3 must be prioritized.

1.  **Look Up and Orient to the Surface**: Keep your right hand raised (to protect your head and prepare to vent your BCD) and place your left hand on your BCD exhaust valve.
2.  **Kick Toward the Surface at a Normal Pace**: Do not sprint. Keep your ascent rate within standard limits (see [[en/50_Safety/Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards|Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards]]).
3.  **Exhale a Continuous "Ah" Sound**: This is the critical step. As you ascend, the gas in your lungs expands. Making a continuous sound keeps your airway open, allowing expanding gas to vent and **preventing pulmonary over-expansion injury (lung rupture $\rightarrow$ AGE)** [1][2].
4.  **Keep the Regulator Mouthpiece in Your Mouth**: As ambient pressure drops during ascent, expanding gas in the cylinder may provide a final breath [2].
5.  **Approach the Surface**: Vent your BCD to control your speed (as BCD gas expands too) and look around your head.
6.  **Surface**: Establish positive buoyancy immediately (inflate your BCD orally or ditch weights) and signal for help.

---

## ⚠️ Physiological Boundaries

*   **Never hold your breath during ascent**: Breath-holding causes Arterial Gas Embolism (AGE). Ascending from 10 meters to the surface while holding your breath doubles the gas volume in your lungs [2]. For AGE symptoms and first aid, see [[en/50_Safety/DCS Symptom Recognition & Oxygen First Aid|DCS Symptom Recognition & Oxygen First Aid]].
*   **CESA is for No-Decompression Dives**: Ascending directly to the surface with a decompression obligation carries a high risk of DCS. This is why technical diving protocols rely on gas redundancy and team procedures to eliminate CESA from the emergency options list.

---

## 🔁 Sidemount Practice Recommendations

*   Recreational sidemount divers should maintain muscle memory by **simulating CESA horizontally in confined water** (swimming at least 9 meters while continuously exhaling, keeping speed within 18 m/min) [1].
*   A higher-value exercise is practicing options 1–3 until they are second nature: **the correct answer in sidemount is to never let yourself end up needing CESA**.
