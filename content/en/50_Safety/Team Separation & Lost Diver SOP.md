# Team Separation & Lost Diver SOP (Lost Buddy & Lost Line Procedures)

Team separation can take two forms: **losing your partner (Lost Buddy)** or **losing the guideline (Lost Line)**. The latter represents one of the most hazardous scenarios in overhead environments. This article serves as a **review for divers certified in cave or technical wreck diving**; open water recreational divers only need to refer to the first section.

---

## 🌊 Open Water Team Separation Standard (Recreational)

1.  Stop and conduct a slow 360-degree visual scan on the spot for **1 minute** (look for bubbles or light beams) [1].
2.  If your partner is not located $\rightarrow$ **ascend slowly to the surface**, controlling your ascent rate (see [[en/50_Safety/Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards|Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards]]).
3.  Wait at the surface for your buddy. If they do not surface within a reasonable time, initiate surface emergency rescue procedures.
4.  **Confirm this protocol during your pre-dive briefing** so that both divers do not spin in circles searching underwater.

---

## 🔦 Lost Buddy in Overhead Environments [2][3]

1.  **Stop and Shield Lights**: Partially cover your primary light lens with your hand and scan the darkness for your buddy's beam. If you locate the beam and they are secure on the line—rejoin them, **abort the dive, and exit to conduct a debrief** [2].
2.  **Place a Reference Marker**: Place a **personalized line arrow** (marked with your name or initials) on the guideline, pointing toward the exit. This serves two functions: a reference point for your own navigation during the search, and a starting point for rescue teams if you fail to return [2][3].
3.  **Evaluate Gas Limits**: Your search gas limit equals **breathing to your turnaround pressure, plus 1/3 of your reserve gas**. (NSS-CDS Example: starting at 3000 psi, turning at 2200 psi $\rightarrow$ search limit is 1700 psi) [3]. **Golden Rule: Never exhaust your own emergency gas reserve to locate someone who may already have turned back** [3].
4.  **Execute a Systematic Search**: Scan along the active passage, **including the ceiling** (a buddy with runaway buoyancy will drift high). Look for fresh silt disturbance at side tunnels [3]. If you must step off the guideline, anchor your safety spool (approx. 45m line) to the primary line first [2].
5.  **Recovering Your Buddy**: Once located, check their pressure and stress levels. **Position them in front of you** and return to the main line to exit [3].
6.  **Terminate if Gas Limit is Reached**: If you reach your search gas limit and have not found your buddy, cease the search, exit, and notify emergency services immediately. This is not abandonment; it is preventing a double-casualty scenario.

## 🧵 Lost Line in Overhead Environments [1][2]

If visibility drops to zero (silt-out) or you become disoriented and lose contact with the guideline:

1.  **STOP**: Any movement can carry you further from the line. Stabilize your buoyancy (hover 30–50 cm off the floor to prevent further silting) and control your breathing.
2.  **Scan for Light**: Shield your primary beam and look for your buddy's light or ambient light from the exit.
3.  **Establish an Anchor**: Locate a secure anchor point (such as a rock protrusion) and **tie off your safety spool**—this is your only reference back to your starting point [1][2].
4.  **Arc Search**: Feed line out from the spool and sweep in a horizontal arc close to the floor, extending your arm. Slowly pay out line to expand the search radius until your hand or fin sweeps the primary guideline [1].
5.  **Locate the Guideline**: Once you find the line, verify your direction of exit (check line arrows—the arrow tip points to the exit), clip your spool line to the guideline, release your anchor, and exit along the line.
6.  **Gas Discipline**: A lost line scenario is a Rock Bottom emergency, and your breathing rate will spike (see the task loading table in [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Consumption Rate (SAC & RMV) Calculation|Gas Consumption Rate (SAC & RMV) Calculation]]). This is the exact reason the 1/3 reserve rule is mandated (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles|Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles]]).

## 🛡 Prevention Strategies

*   **Team Formation Discipline**: Maintain a 2–3m spacing along the line and ensure light beams are visible to teammates (see [[en/30_Skills/Team Hand Signals, Light & Touch Communication|Team Hand Signals, Light & Touch Communication]]).
*   **Tactile Contact on Silt-Out**: Establish tactile contact on the line as soon as visibility begins to deteriorate, rather than waiting for a total blackout.
*   **Individual Gear Standard**: Every technical diver must carry at least one personalized line arrow, one safety spool, and two backup lights [2].
