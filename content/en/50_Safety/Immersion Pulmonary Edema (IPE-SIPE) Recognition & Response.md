# Immersion Pulmonary Edema (IPE-SIPE) Recognition & Response (Immersion Pulmonary Edema)

**Immersion Pulmonary Edema (IPE; referred to as SIPE in swimmers)** is a sudden, high-risk clinical event receiving close attention in modern diving medicine. Key symptoms include **shortness of breath underwater, coughing up pink/blood-tinged frothy sputum, and a sensation of drowning ("water in the lungs")**. It is frequently misdiagnosed as water inhalation, mask flooding, or panic, which delays life-saving care. While this site covers DCS and hypothermia elsewhere, the physiological mechanism of IPE is completely different—**it is independent of depth and can strike otherwise healthy individuals**. This article addresses this critical gap.

> ⚠️ **Safety Notice**: This article serves as an educational review of SIPE recognition and response and does not replace medical consultation. Any unexplained breathing difficulty underwater or post-dive must be treated as a medical emergency.

---

## 🫁 1. Physiological Mechanism: Why Immersion Causes Lung Fluid

IPE **is not caused by inhaling water; it is a hydrostatic leakage of fluid across the alveolar-capillary membrane** [1][2]:

*   **Immersion & Cold Exposure**: Submerging in water, particularly cold water, causes blood vessels in the skin and extremities to constrict. This shifts blood toward the chest core, **increasing thoracic blood volume and pulmonary artery pressure**.
*   **Capillary Overload**: If this pressure shift is combined with **physical exertion, pre-existing hypertension, or reduced cardiac compliance**, pressure inside the pulmonary capillaries exceeds a critical threshold. Blood plasma leaks into the air sacs (alveoli), reducing the surface area available for gas exchange [1][3].
*   **Independent of Depth**: SIPE can strike at any depth—even in a shallow swimming pool—unlike DCS or nitrogen narcosis, which escalate systematically with depth [1].

## 🚨 2. Symptom Identification (The Most Common Misdiagnosis)

| Symptom | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Dyspnea / Shortness of breath** | The core symptom; rapid breathing that does not resolve with rest and worsens as the dive continues [1][2]. |
| **Coughing / Pink frothy sputum** | The hallmark sign of alveolar fluid leakage (hemoptysis) [1][2]. |
| **Chest tightness / Sensation of drowning** | Divers frequently describe the sensation as "trying to breathe water" [1]. |
| **Disproportionate fatigue or weakness** | Caused by a rapid drop in gas exchange efficiency [1]. |
| **Hypoxia despite a functioning regulator** | **Critical Diagnostic Clue**: If the gas supply is confirmed open and functioning, but the diver continues to experience air hunger, suspect IPE. |

> 📌 **Differential Diagnosis**: First exclude gas or equipment issues (refer to [[en/50_Safety/Sidemount Regulator Malfunction & Leak Management|Sidemount Regulator Malfunction & Leak Management]] and [[en/50_Safety/Out of Gas (OOG) & Long Hose Sharing Procedure|Out of Gas (OOG) & Long Hose Sharing Procedure]]). If your gas supply is functioning normally but the diver continues to struggle and cough, **do not dismiss it as panic or water inhalation; manage it as IPE**.

## 🧯 3. Field Response SOP [1][4]

1.  **Signal Buddy & Abort Dive Immediately**: IPE can escalate rapidly; terminate the dive and get the diver out of the water as quickly as possible.
2.  **Controlled Ascent; Do Not Ascent Rapidly**: Maintain a standard rate of ascent (see [[en/50_Safety/Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards|Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards]]). If you have a decompression obligation, balance decompression requirements against worsening hypoxia; prioritize airway safety and seek immediate medical help.
3.  **Minimize Workload**: Once out of the water, ensure the diver rests completely. Avoid carrying gear or swimming, as **physical exertion exacerbates capillary leakage**. Assist the diver using towing techniques if needed (see [[en/50_Safety/Unconscious Diver Rescue & Towing Procedures|Unconscious Diver Rescue & Towing Procedures]]).
4.  **Administer 100% Oxygen**: Use the same emergency oxygen kit configured for DCS first aid (see [[en/50_Safety/DCS Symptom Recognition & Oxygen First Aid|DCS Symptom Recognition & Oxygen First Aid]]). Keep the diver in a **semi-seated position**, which makes breathing easier than lying flat [4].
5.  **Evacuate to Medical Care**: Even if symptoms resolve rapidly on land, **the diver must be evaluated at a hospital**—IPE can recur, and underlying cardiac factors must be ruled out [1][3].

> ❌ **Field Contraindications**: Do not allow the diver to rest and perform a second dive. Do not force them to lie flat, and do not treat it as simple water aspiration.

## 🛡 4. Risk Factors & Prevention (Sidemount & Tech Perspectives)

Case-control studies identify the following independent risk factors: **age > 50, female gender, pre-dive use of NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs), depths > 20 m, heavy physical exertion, daily medications, and (uncontrolled) hypertension** [3].

Controllable prevention measures for sidemount and technical diving:
1.  **Proper Weighting & Buoyancy**: Over-weighting combined with poor BCD management increases physical exertion, elevating risk. Perform weight checks to manage trim and reduce energy expenditure (see [[en/30_Skills/In-Water Buoyancy & Horizontal Trim|In-Water Buoyancy & Horizontal Trim]] and [[en/20_Equipment/Exposure Suit Selection & Trim Influence|Exposure Suits Selection & Buoyancy Trim Impact]]) [1].
2.  **Avoid Pre-Dive Overhydration & NSAIDs**: Excessive pre-dive fluid consumption and anti-inflammatory medications are identified risk factors [3].
3.  **Manage Exertion & Currents**: Swimming against strong currents causes chest pressure to spike. Use DPVs or plan routes to minimize exertion (see [[en/60_Advanced/DPV Scooter & Sidemount Integration|DPV Scooter & Sidemount Integration]]).
4.  **Thermal Protection**: Cold exposure is a primary trigger (see [[en/50_Safety/Hypothermia Prevention & Management|Hypothermia Prevention & Management]]).
5.  **Medical Evaluations**: Divers over 50 or those with a history of hypertension or cardiac conditions should obtain a diving medical clearance before technical training [1][3].

> 📌 **Note**: There is currently **no screening tool to predict individual susceptibility to SIPE** [1]. Recognition of symptoms and immediate evacuation are your primary lines of defense.
