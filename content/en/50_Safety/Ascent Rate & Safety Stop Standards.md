# Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards (Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards)

While multiple articles (such as Rock Bottom reserves, buoyancy failures, and valve drills) refer to "ascending at a normal rate and executing a safety stop," this article formally defines those parameters. It standardizes guidelines for **ascent rates, safety stops, decompression stops, and deep stops** for cross-referencing across other articles on this site.

> ⚠️ **Safety Notice**: Specific parameters vary by training agency and computer algorithm. Always follow your active dive computer and your training agency's standards.

---

## ⬆️ 1. Ascent Rates

| Ascent Phase | Recommended Rate | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Standard Ascent (Bottom to stops)** | **9–10 m/min (30 ft/min)** | Current mainstream technical standard. Half the speed of older 18 m/min rates, reducing DCS risk [1][2]. |
| **Shallow Zone (Last 10 m to surface)** | **Reduce to 3–6 m/min** | The shallowest zone has the largest relative volume and pressure changes, presenting the highest risk of bubble formation [1][2]. |
| **Decompression Stop Intervals** | **3 m/min** (or per computer) | Slow, controlled movement between consecutive decompression stops [3]. |

*   **Physical Mechanism**: Ascending too rapidly does not allow dissolved nitrogen sufficient time to be exhaled through the lungs, causing gas to seed bubble growth in blood and tissues $\rightarrow$ Decompression Sickness (DCS). Holding your breath during ascent can cause Pulmonary Over-Inflation Syndrome (POIS) or Arterial Gas Embolism (AGE).
*   **Sidemount Specifics**: When swimming up during a buoyancy emergency (see [[en/50_Safety/BCD & Buoyancy Failure Emergency Procedures|BCD & Buoyancy Failure Emergency Procedures]]), **you must actively control your rate of ascent**, compensating for the positive buoyancy of emptying aluminum cylinders and drysuit air expansion.

---

## ⏸️ 2. Safety Stops

*   **Standard Protocol**: **Perform a 3-minute stop at 5 meters (15 ft)** (acceptable range: 3–6 m). Highly recommended for all dives exceeding ~10 meters or approaching No-Decompression Limits (NDLs) [1][2].
*   **Purpose**: Continues offgassing under a low pressure gradient, cushions ascent rate errors, and stabilizes team formation.
*   **Sidemount Practice**: Hover in a horizontal Trim at 5 m. Because aluminum cylinders become positive as they empty, vent your BCD fully and prepare to slide your sliding D-rings forward (see [[en/30_Skills/In-Water Cylinder Management Techniques|In-Water Cylinder Management Techniques]]).

---

## 🧮 3. Decompression Stops

*   Exceeding your NDL triggers **mandatory decompression stops**. You must stop at depths specified by your computer or decompression schedule; **do not ascend directly to the surface**.
*   Using Enriched Air Nitrox or pure oxygen at appropriate MODs accelerates nitrogen elimination during decompression stops (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Sidemount Gas Switching Procedures|Sidemount Gas Switching Procedures]] and [[en/40_Gas_Management/Oxygen Exposure & Nitrogen Narcosis Management|Oxygen Exposure & Nitrogen Narcosis Management]]).
*   **Rock Bottom gas reserves must cover your entire decompression obligation plus solve time** (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles|Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles]]).

---

## 🌀 4. The "Deep Stop" Controversy

*   In previous decades, inserting "deep stops" (stops calculated between maximum depth and your first shallow decompression stop) was popular.
*   **Recent Studies (e.g., US Navy NEDU 2011)**: Research demonstrates that **deep stops do not reduce DCS risk and may actually increase slow-tissue gas loading** under certain profiles [4].
*   **Current Consensus**: Most modern algorithms (such as Bühlmann ZHL-16C with Gradient Factors) incorporate safety margins by adjusting GF values rather than adding arbitrary deep stops. **Do not add manual deep stops unless directed by your computer or training agency** [3][4].
