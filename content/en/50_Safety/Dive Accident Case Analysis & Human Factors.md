# Dive Accident Case Analysis & Human Factors (Human Factors & Incident Analysis)

In sidemount and technical diving, accidents are rarely caused by a single equipment failure. Instead, they are almost always the **critical result of a sequence of minor human errors and decision-making biases compounding over time**.

Human Factors research (such as Gareth Lock's *The Human Diver* framework) emphasizes that understanding a diver's cognitive limits under stress, loss of Situational Awareness (SA), and team dynamics is the true key to preventing fatal accidents [1][2].

This article introduces core human factors concepts and conducts in-depth analyses of three real-world sidemount accidents to extract critical safety lessons.

---

## 🧠 1. Core Concepts of Human Factors (Human Factors Framework)

In technical diving decision-making, divers are highly susceptible to the following three psychological and cognitive traps:

1.  **Loss of Situational Awareness (SA)**:
    *   The diver's perception of their current state (depth, cylinder pressure, guideline position, and buddy location) diverges from reality. Under high task loading, the brain experiences "tunnel vision," focusing on a single issue (e.g., camera settings or untangling a line) while completely ignoring gas consumption or depth drift [1][3].
2.  **Task Loading and Cognitive Overload**:
    *   When multiple tasks must be managed simultaneously underwater (e.g., maintaining horizontal trim + navigating a guideline + monitoring gas + adjusting sliding D-rings + managing a camera), the diver's cognitive resources are exhausted. At this point, a minor issue (e.g., a flooded mask) can easily trigger a panic loop [2][4].
3.  **Cognitive Biases**:
    *   **Normalization of Deviance**: Repeatedly violating safety protocols (such as not analyzing gas mixtures or allowing excessive pressure offsets before switching regulators) without experiencing an accident leads the brain to perceive the shortcut as safe, until a critical threshold is breached [1][5].
    *   **Sunk Cost Fallacy**: Continuing a dive despite feeling unwell or encountering strong currents because of the expensive travel costs or effort spent getting to the dive site [5].

## 🚨 2. Case Studies of Real-World Sidemount Accidents

### 🔴 Case 1: Canister Light Cable Entanglement in a Cave
*   **Incident Profile**:
    A sidemount diver traversing a narrow cave passage suddenly found himself unable to move forward. The diver attempted to force his way forward rather than stopping to assess the situation. The struggle caused his left cylinder valve to snag on a loose cable, causing the handwheel to twist and shear the low-pressure regulator hose, venting gas rapidly. Because the passage was too narrow, his buddy could not swim side-by-side to share gas. The diver panicked, struggled with his harness, and drowned before reaching the exit.
*   **Human Factors & Mechanical Analysis**:
    1.  **Improper Gear Configuration**: The diver was using a canister light with a battery pack but **failed to stow the excess power cable under harness rubber bands**, leaving a loose loop of cable dangling along his left side that caught on the wall [6]. (For standard light routing, see [[en/20_Equipment/Primary Light and Goodman Handle Configuration|Dive Light Systems & Goodman Handle Configuration]]).
    2.  **Incorrect Reaction to Resistance**: When encountering physical resistance in a restricted space, the correct immediate response is **"Stop, Breathe, Think, Act."** Divers should trace the snag or back up to relieve tension. In a state of lost situational awareness, the diver instinctively kicked harder, which broke his gas regulator [6].
    3.  **Communication & Formation Failure**: Sharing gas in a restriction requires a single-file formation (see [[en/50_Safety/Out of Gas (OOG) & Long Hose Sharing Procedure|Out of Gas (OOG) & Long Hose Sharing Procedure]]), but due to panic, the divers failed to establish tactile contact, preventing a successful gas share.

### 🔴 Case 2: Ignoring 1/6 Pressure Switches Resulting in Gas Depletion
*   **Incident Profile**:
    Two technical divers conducted a decompression dive to 40 meters carrying dual AL80 aluminum cylinders. Diver A found switching regulators tedious and decided to breathe his right cylinder down to 80 bar before switching to his left. When the right cylinder reached 90 bar, the right first stage froze in the cold water, triggering a continuous free-flow. Diver A shut down the right valve, only to find that his left cylinder pressure was at 50 bar (as he had consumed most of the left cylinder during the descent and swim). The remaining 50 bar was far below the team's Rock Bottom reserve for that depth. Both divers ran out of gas during decompression stops at 15 meters, resulting in severe decompression sickness.
*   **Human Factors & Mechanical Analysis**:
    1.  **Normalization of Deviance**: Diver A was accustomed to single-tank recreational diving and assumed that "as long as total gas volume is sufficient, the switch timing does not matter." He ignored the fact that **sidemount cylinders are completely independent and each must independently maintain self-rescue redundancy** [7].
    2.  **Disregarding Rock Bottom Priorities**: Neither cylinder contained sufficient gas to support a two-diver emergency ascent (see calculations in [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles|Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles]]). Once the right cylinder failed, the remaining left cylinder was insufficient to cover decompression stops [7].

### 🔴 Case 3: Sliding D-ring Dislodgement Causing Trim Failure
*   **Incident Profile**:
    A student practicing cylinder manipulation and sliding D-ring adjustments pulled too hard on a lower bolt snap. Because the sliding D-ring was not locked securely to his waist belt, it pulled off completely and sank into the silt. The bottom of the right cylinder lost its anchor point, and the negative weight of the steel tank pulled the diver's right side down. To maintain a horizontal trim, the diver over-exerted his core muscles, leading to hypercapnia (CO₂ retention). In his rush to search the silt for the lost D-ring, he lost track of his depth, ascending from 18 meters to 8 meters and risking an uncontrolled decompression ascent.
*   **Human Factors & Mechanical Analysis**:
    1.  **Task Loading Overload**: The student was managing three issues simultaneously: a floating cylinder, a lateral trim offset, and a lost hardware search. This exhausted his cognitive resources, causing him to fail at **depth monitoring (situational awareness)** [8].
    2.  **Failure to Deploy Backup Fixes**: When a sliding D-ring fails, the diver should immediately clip the lower bolt snap to a rear waist D-ring, a crotch strap D-ring, or secure the cylinder between his thighs to stabilize trim first, rather than searching the silt while unstable [8].

## 🛠 3. In-Water Risk Management Guidelines

To manage human factor risks in sidemount diving, teams should adhere to the following principles:

1.  **Establish Psychological Safety**:
    *   Ensure every team member (regardless of experience) has the right to abort the dive (Call the dive) at any point, for any reason (e.g., cold, gear anomalies, or feeling uncomfortable), without facing judgment or criticism from teammates [2][5].
2.  **Execute Pre-Dive Briefings and Gear Checks (GUE EDGE / S-Drill)**:
    *   Verify that you can reach both cylinder valves, regulators, and cutting tools, and check that hoses and cables run cleanly without crossing (see [[en/70_Resources/Pre-Dive Checklists|Pre-Dive Checklists]]) [1].
3.  **Strictly Adhere to the 1/6 Pressure Switch Rule (or 30-bar Rotation)**:
    *   This protocol is essential not only for lateral trim but to ensure that **if one cylinder fails at any point, the remaining cylinder holds sufficient Rock Bottom gas to support both divers** [7].
