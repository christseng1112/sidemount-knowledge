# DPV Scooter & Sidemount Integration (DPV Integration with Sidemount)

DPV (Diver Propulsion Vehicle) technology exponentially extends the penetration range of cave and wreck diving, while simultaneously rewriting gas planning, trim management, and emergency procedures. Sidemount + DPV is the dominant configuration for long-range cave traversals—the flat cross-sectional profile of sidemount aligns perfectly with the low-drag requirements of DPV operation.

> ⚠️ DPV use requires formal training (e.g., TDI DPV or Cave DPV courses). This article serves as a conceptual overview only [1].

---

## 🔗 Tow System Configuration

*   **Tow Cord on Crotch Strap D-ring**: High-performance DPVs are towed via a cord clipped to the **crotch strap D-ring**, transferring all towing force to the harness. Arms handle steering only, bearing no load [1][2]. Sidemount harnesses come with a standard crotch strap, making this integration natural.
*   **Tow Point Geometry** (community convention) [3]:
    *   **12/6 Layout** (clip points above/below the handle): Pitch-stable, the DPV "flies itself." Ideal for long straight-line cruising.
    *   **3/9 Layout** (clip points left/right of the handle): Pitch-unstable, requiring continuous arm input, but highly maneuverable. Ideal for multi-bend passages.
*   **Body Position**: Maintain horizontal trim while being towed. Fins serve as rudders. Dual sidemount cylinders provide superior tow stability compared to backmount doubles in the DPV wake.

---

## ⚖️ DPV Trim & Buoyancy

*   The DPV itself should be trimmed to **neutral buoyancy and horizontal hover**: Adjust using the internal ballast compartment (add lead or foam) [1][4].
*   A DPV with noticeable positive or negative buoyancy will directly contaminate the diver's own buoyancy control [1].
*   Transitioning between saltwater and freshwater (e.g., ocean-connected caves) requires re-trimming [4].

## 🧮 Gas & Distance Planning

DPVs do not change your breathing rate—they change **distance**. This shifts gas planning from time-based to "can I swim back?":

1.  **DPV Failure = Fin Swim Home**: The planning baseline is gas required to **swim out from the failure point on fins alone**. Penetration distance is calculated using "swim-back 1/3," not "DPV 1/3" (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles|Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles]]).
2.  **Battery Rule**: Follows the same 1/3 logic as gas—turn back after consuming 1/3 of battery capacity, retaining a failure margin.
3.  **Multi-Cylinder Coordination**: Long DPV traversals commonly pair with stage cylinders. For mounting order, see [[en/60_Advanced/Multi-Cylinder & Decompression Stage Configuration|Multi-Cylinder & Decompression Stage Configuration]].

## 🚨 DPV-Specific Emergency Scenarios

| Scenario | Response |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Stuck Throttle (Runaway)** | Release the handle; let the tow cord absorb the force. Angle the nose up to decelerate. Cut the power switch. Do not fight the machine. |
| **Rapid Depth Change** | DPV speed causes ascent/descent rates far exceeding fin swimming. **Monitor ear equalization and computer warnings**. Decelerate during ascent phases (see [[en/50_Safety/Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards|Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards]]). |
| **DPV Failure (Abandon)** | A neutrally trimmed DPV can be clipped to the guideline and marked. Fin-swim out. This is another reason to trim the DPV to neutral. |
| **Buddy Separation** | DPV speeds can instantly extend team spacing. Formation discipline and light signals (see [[en/30_Skills/Team Hand Signals, Light & Touch Communication|Team Hand Signals, Light & Touch Communication]]) must be **stricter than fin-swimming dives**. |
