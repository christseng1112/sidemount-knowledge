# Solo Diving & Extreme No-Mount Configuration (Solo & No-Mount Rigging)

Sidemount diving originated from British cave exploration, where divers developed cylinder-removal techniques to pass through extremely narrow passages. When a passage is smaller than the diver's torso plus mounted cylinders, **No-Mount** becomes the only option.

Simultaneously, in extreme exploration environments or under specific safety philosophies, **Solo Diving (Self-Sufficient Diving)** demands the highest levels of equipment redundancy and configuration discipline [1][2]. This article covers both extreme techniques in detail.

---

## 📐 1. Extreme No-Mount Underwater Procedures

No-Mount refers to **temporarily unclipping cylinders from the harness and pushing them forward by hand, or carrying them completely detached, to transit through extreme restrictions** [1][3].

```
【No-Mount Restriction Passage Sequence】
  - Assess passage & route guideline
  - Unclip bottom bolt snap ──> Release neck bungee ──> Push cylinder(s) forward ──> Re-mount after passing
```

### 🛠 Equipment Requirements
*   **Bungee System**: Must use a bungee system that can be blindly released and re-attached underwater (e.g., Loop Bungee or quick-release bolt snap bungee).
*   **Hose Length**: The right-side 2.1m long hose and left short hose must have sufficient slack to allow cylinder repositioning without pulling the regulator from your mouth [3][4].

### 🏊 Standard Underwater Removal Steps
1.  **Hover & Assess**: Hover at the restriction entrance, verify structural integrity, and confirm with your buddy via hand signals.
2.  **Unclip Bottom**: Single-handedly locate and release the bottom bolt snap. The cylinder tail will now float free.
3.  **Release Neck Bungee**: Reach under your armpit and slide the bungee off the valve handwheel.
4.  **Forward Push**: Grip the valve or first stage guard with your right hand and push the cylinder flat against your abdomen, swimming behind it [1][3].
    *   *Warning*: Never use regulator hoses as load-bearing pull points.
5.  **Single vs. Dual Removal**: For narrow passages, push one cylinder (single-side no-mount). For passages narrower than shoulder width, detach both cylinders and push one in each hand in a "superman" posture [3][4].
6.  **Recovery**: After clearing the restriction, hover in a wider area and re-mount: attach neck bungee first, then clip the bottom bolt snap to the sliding D-ring. Perform a flow check [1].

## 🚨 2. Solo Diving: The Rule of Three (Triple Redundancy)

Solo diving means **no one is available underwater to assist in an emergency**. All survival-critical equipment must follow the "Rule of Three" principle, ensuring that even after two independent failures, a backup remains available [2][5].

### 🧯 Core Equipment Triple Redundancy Matrix

| Equipment Category | Primary | Secondary | Tertiary |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Breathing Gas** | Right cylinder (independent) [1] | Left cylinder (independent) [1] | Deco/Bailout cylinder (depth-dependent) |
| **Lighting** | Primary light (Canister/Goodman) | Backup light #1 (right shoulder) | Backup light #2 (left shoulder) [5] |
| **Cutting Tools** | Primary knife/shears (waist belt) | Line cutter (wrist strap or shoulder) | Shears (thigh pocket) [5] |
| **Navigation/Monitoring** | Primary dive computer (wireless AI) | Backup computer / bottom timer | Compass (mechanical, independent) |
| **Mask** | Primary mask (worn) | Backup low-volume mask (thigh pocket) | High-retention/anti-fog primary (solo: no buddy backup) |

*   **Gas Independence (Natural Sidemount Advantage)**: Sidemount's inherent dual-cylinder independence is the perfect foundation for solo diving. If one first stage ruptures, the other side provides 100% independent gas and a regulator. **However, solo divers must strictly maintain 1/3 gas reserves; otherwise, a single-side loss leaves insufficient gas for a solo ascent** [2][6].
*   **Reverse Block Contingency**: During solo diving, if a reverse block occurs, no one can monitor your gas. Carry a backup slate and configure your dive computer with strict gas alarms [6].

## 🛡 3. Abrasion Protection & Butt-Slider Techniques

In extreme caves and restrictions, lightweight bladders are easily punctured by stalactites or sharp rock walls [3][7].

### 1. Armored Wing (Bladder Protection)
*   Select bladders with **1000D to 2000D double-layer military-grade Cordura** outer shells (e.g., XDEEP Stealth Tec/Classic or ballistic nylon heavy-duty sidemount systems) [7].
*   Avoid ultra-lightweight single-layer travel bladders in cave environments.

### 2. Butt-Slider / Butt-Plate Protector
*   **Purpose**: When crawling along silty floors or squeezing through passages with your back against the ceiling, the buttocks and thighs experience the most abrasion [3][7].
*   **Configuration**: Mount a metal or plastic **butt-slider** at the harness seat position to protect the metal backplate, webbing screws, and hardware from grinding damage. Apply neoprene abrasion patches to the thigh and seat areas of your exposure suit [7].
