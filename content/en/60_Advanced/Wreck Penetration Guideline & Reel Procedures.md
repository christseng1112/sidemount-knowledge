# Wreck Penetration Guideline & Reel Procedures (Wreck Penetration Line & Reel Procedures)

> ⚠️ **This article is intended for review by divers certified in Advanced Wreck or Cave diving.** Wreck interiors are a more hazardous overhead environment than caves: structures collapse, rust particles (rust snow) cause blackouts faster than silt, and sharp edges are everywhere.

This article focuses on **guideline procedures**: primary reels, tie-offs, and Jump/Gap spool operations. For general overhead line routing and light signals, see [[en/60_Advanced/Sidemount Guidelines & Operations in Overhead Environments|Sidemount Guidelines & Operations in Overhead Environments]].

---

## 🧵 Reel & Spool Assignments

| Tool | Line Length | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Reel** | 100 m+ | Lay the main guideline from open water into the wreck [1][2]. |
| **Jump/Gap Spool** | 15–30 m | Bridge gaps between existing lines or jump to side passages [2]. |
| **Safety Spool** | ~45 m | Dedicated to lost line / lost buddy searches (see [[en/50_Safety/Team Separation & Lost Diver SOP|Team Separation & Lost Diver SOP]]). |

*   **The reel diver is always first in the team**: They lay line on entry and recover line on exit—**First in, Last out** [1].

## 📐 Tie-off Standard Operating Procedures

### 1. Primary Tie-off
*   **Location**: In **open water outside the wreck structure**, with a direct, unobstructed path to the surface [1][3].
*   **Technique**: **Two wraps** around the anchor point, then lock off. Select sturdy, fixed attachment points with no sharp edges [1].
*   **Depth**: Set deep enough to avoid being disturbed by surface activity or wave surge [1].

### 2. Secondary Tie-off
*   **Set immediately upon entering the overhead zone**: This serves as a backup if the primary tie-off is severed, cut, or abraded [1][3].
*   From this point inward, add **placement tie-offs** at every directional change or long straight section (every 3–8 m depending on visibility). Keep the line flush against structures; never allow it to span open space (mid-water lines are entanglement traps).

### 3. Line Laying Discipline
*   The line must be **taut, wall-hugging, and on the same side of the team's travel path**. A slack line is more dangerous than no line (entanglement risks are covered in [[en/60_Advanced/Sidemount Guidelines & Operations in Overhead Environments|Sidemount Guidelines & Operations in Overhead Environments]]).
*   Buoyancy control and kick technique during line laying (frog kick to prevent rust disturbance; see [[en/30_Skills/Sidemount Propulsion Techniques|Sidemount Propulsion Techniques]]) are primary assessment criteria in wreck courses [2].

## 🔀 Jump / Gap Operations

Wrecks often have pre-existing permanent lines left by previous teams. Standard jump procedures [2][3]:

1.  **Jump (Side Passage)**: Tie one end of a spool to the main line, **place a personalized directional line arrow pointing toward the exit**, then run the spool to the side passage and tie off—creating a continuous, followable path.
2.  **Gap (Bridging a Break)**: Where two existing lines are disconnected, bridge the gap with a spool, securing both ends.
3.  **Rule**: **The guideline must be continuous from start to finish**. Any "swim a short gap without a line" section becomes a fatal break point in zero visibility.
4.  Recover your own jump spools and line arrows on exit. **Never remove another team's permanent lines**.

## 🚪 Penetration Decision Framework (Think Before You Enter)

1.  **Sidemount Advantage**: Low-clearance deck layers and cable-dense areas—sidemount allows unclipping and pushing a single cylinder through (see [[en/60_Advanced/No-Mount & Stage Dropping Techniques|No-Mount & Stage Dropping Techniques]]). However, **cylinder removal for penetration must be a planned action, not an improvised decision**.
2.  **Gas Planning**: Calculate penetration gas using the 1/3 rule. For rust-out blackout scenarios, validate exit gas using high-workload RMV values (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles|Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles]]).
3.  **Structural Risk**: Wrecks shift—reassess structural stability after tidal flows and storms. Overhead deck collapse is a hazard type that caves do not present [3].
4.  **Abort Authority**: Any team member may "Thumb the dive" (abort) and the entire team exits (see [[en/30_Skills/Team Hand Signals, Light & Touch Communication|Team Hand Signals, Light & Touch Communication]]).
