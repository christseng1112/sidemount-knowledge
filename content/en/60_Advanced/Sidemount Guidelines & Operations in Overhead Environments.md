# Sidemount Guidelines & Operations in Overhead Environments (Overhead Environments: Line Work & Comm)

Overhead environments (caves and wrecks) are both the origin and the most demanding application of sidemount diving. In environments where direct vertical ascent is impossible and visibility can drop to zero from silt-out at any moment, **continuous guideline management (Line Work), strict anti-snag routing, and a silent communication system** are the factors that determine survival [1][2].

This article examines overhead sidemount operations across three dimensions: guideline laying procedures, anti-snag streamlining, and light/hand/touch communication protocols.

---

## 🧭 1. Cave & Wreck Guideline Management (Line Work & Reels)

The guideline (Cave Line) is the diver's only lifeline to the exit in an overhead environment.

```
【Standard Guideline Laying Diagram】
  [ Open Water ] ──(Primary Tie-off)──> [ Cave Entrance / Wreck Opening ] ──(Secondary Tie-off)──> [ Deep Overhead ]
                                                                              │ (Routed along one side of the passage floor)
                                                                              ▼ (Never crossing the passage center)
```

### Laying Principles & Tie-offs
*   **Primary Tie-off**: Must be set in **open water** outside the overhead environment (e.g., in the sunlight zone, on an exterior rock or anchor chain). This protects against silt-out or collapse blocking the entrance [1].
*   **Secondary Tie-off**: The first tie-off after entering the overhead zone, serving as a backup in case the primary is severed by boats, waves, or bystanders [1].
*   **Tension Control & Routing**:
    *   The line must be kept taut during deployment. Slack lines create loops that easily entangle cylinder valves or fins [1].
    *   The guideline should be routed along **one edge of the passage floor**. Crossing the passage center or creating intersections in narrow passages is strictly prohibited (this blocks teammates and risks being severed by cylinders) [1][3].
*   **Sidemount Advantage in Line Work**: Because the sidemount diver's chest is completely clear (no bulky backplate or manifold), operating a reel with arms extended forward provides an unobstructed field of vision and perfect horizontal trim [3].

## 🛡 2. Anti-Snag Streamlining in Overhead Environments (Snag Prevention)

In low-clearance passages, sharp rock projections (stalactites) and wreck debris can catch equipment at any moment. Sidemount gear must be "zero-snag" optimized [2]:

1.  **Valve Protection & Stowage**: First stages and valve handwheels must be completely tucked under the armpits. Use neck bungees to pull cylinder necks tight. While swimming, **arms should be held slightly inward** to shield the valve area from ceiling impacts [2][3].
2.  **Hose Routing (Zero Loops)**:
    *   The right-side 2.1m long hose must run flush along the neck and torso. Excess hose is stowed neatly under cylinder bungees [2].
    *   BCD and drysuit low-pressure inflator hoses must be precisely sized. **No hose loop large enough to insert a hand** may exist across the chest or abdomen [2][3].
3.  **Accessories Flush-Mounted**: Spools, backup lights, and double-ended bolt snaps must be flush against the harness webbing. Zero dangling equipment is permitted. All accessories follow the **"one-hand release, two-hand access"** principle [2].

## 💬 3. Silent Communication Systems (Light, Hand & Touch)

In overhead environments, verbal communication is impossible, and teammates are typically arranged in single file. Communication must be highly efficient and standardized:

### 🔦 Light Signals (First Line of Communication)
The primary light serves as both illumination and the fastest passive communication tool. The beam should always be projected on the floor ahead (within your buddy's field of view) [4][5]:
*   **OK (Circle)**: Trace a slow, even circle on the floor with the beam. The receiving diver responds with the same circle.
*   **Attention (Slow sweep)**: Move the beam slowly left-right or up-down on the floor. Means "look at me, I need to communicate" or to direct attention to specific obstacles [4][5].
*   **Emergency (Rapid shaking)**: Shake the beam rapidly and erratically. Means "serious emergency, assist immediately!" The buddy must turn and provide gas or support immediately [4][5].

### 🤝 One-Handed Hand Signals
Because one hand is typically holding the guideline or a light, sidemount divers must use **single-handed signals**.
*   *Technique*: When signaling, illuminate your signaling hand with your **own light beam** to ensure visibility in the dark. Never shine the beam directly into your buddy's eyes (causes temporary blindness) [4][5].

### 🖐️ Touch Contact Communication (Zero-Visibility / Silt-Out)
When silt-out reduces visibility to zero or primary lights fail completely, visual communication is impossible. Both divers must **grab each other's arm or shoulder webbing and communicate via tactile signals** [5][6]:

| Touch Action | Meaning | Field Application |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Push forward** | **Go** | Direct the lead diver to continue along the guideline [5]. |
| **Pull back** | **Reverse** | Signal an obstacle or direct the lead diver to back up [5]. |
| **Squeeze and hold** | **Stop** | Halt immediately and wait for the next instruction [5]. |
| **Rapid repeated squeezes** | **Emergency / Gas problem** | Signal an urgent situation (e.g., out of gas or severe entanglement); initiate gas sharing [5][6]. |
