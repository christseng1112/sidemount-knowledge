# Primary Light and Goodman Handle Configuration (Dive Light Systems)

The execution of light signals outlined in [[en/30_Skills/Team Hand Signals, Light & Touch Communication|Team Hand Signals, Light & Touch Communication]] depends on every diver carrying a primary light that produces a **collimated beam, is securely positioned, and leaves hands free**. In overhead environments, dive lights are not accessories; they are **life support systems**. The primary light is your communication and navigation tool, while backup torches are your insurance to exit in the dark [1][2].

---

## 🔦 The Rule of Thirds for Lights

Standard overhead environment lighting protocol: **1 primary light + at least 2 backup lights** (a total of three independent light sources) [1][2].

*   The logic mirrors gas management: if any single light source fails, two layers of redundancy remain to facilitate a safe exit.
*   **Primary Light Failure = Immediate Abort**: Once you switch to a backup light, head directly for the exit. Backup lights are designed for exiting, not for continuing exploration [1].
*   For open-water night diving, the protocol can be simplified to 1 primary + 1 backup, but the signal and stowage logic remains the same.

## 💡 The Primary Light

### Configuration Styles

| Style | Pros | Cons | Sidemount Compatibility |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Canister Light** | Long burn time, lightweight light head, manageable cord routing. | Canister requires hip mounting, where space is already crowded. | Canister positioning can conflict with cylinder mounting, requiring mounting further back or low on the spine. |
| **Cordless Handheld** | Wireless, easy to switch hands, travel-friendly. | Smaller battery capacity, heavier light head. | **The modern preferred choice for most sidemount divers.** |
| **Helmet Mounted** | Hands-free, beam follows line of sight. | Cannot make hand-independent signals; blinds teammates easily. | Specialized choice for tight restriction/no-mount environments [3]. |

### Optical Specifications
*   **Narrow Beam (6–12°) with Hot Spot**: Effective light communication relies on a defined hot spot—wide-angle video lights cannot produce readable signaling patterns.
*   **Optimal Brightness**: 1000+ lumens is sufficient in clear caves. Beyond raw lumens, **consistent color temperature and honest burn-time indicators** are more critical.
*   **Burn Time**: Must be at least **1.5 times the planned dive time**, verified by actual burn testing rather than trusting manufacturer labels.

### The Goodman Handle
*   A **flat, rigid** plate handle is the standard Goodman handle design. It secures the torch to the back of the hand, leaving all five fingers free to operate reels, D-rings, and cylinders [3].
*   Soft elastic mounts allow for quick hand swapping but lack directional stability and cause fatigue during extended reel operations.
*   Attach a bolt snap to the left side of the handle: clip the light head to the right chest D-ring when not in use to prevent it from dangling when passing through tight restrictions.

## 🔋 Backup Lights

*   **Burn Time Over Brightness**: Exiting a cave from the furthest penetration point can take over an hour; backup torches should offer at least 5 hours of burn time on a medium setting [1].
*   **Stowage Location**: Secured flat against the chest harness webbing under elastic loops (Snoopy loops) with a bolt snap, light head pointing downward—one on the left, one on the right, reachable by either hand [1][2]. A second backup light is often stowed in a pocket [1].
*   **Battery Protocol**: Disposable alkaline or lithium cells are preferred over rechargeable batteries (lower self-discharge rates and predictable discharge curves) [2]; test all backup lights before every dive trip.
*   **Backup lights are never used for communication**—turning on a backup light is a signal that your primary light has failed and you are exiting the dive.

---

## 🧷 Integration Guidelines for Sidemount

1.  **Light Cord Routing (Canister Lights)**: When the canister is mounted on the right hip, the cord routes across the back to the left hand—never across the chest, where it would interfere with long hose deployment (see [[en/20_Equipment/Sidemount Regulator & Hose Routing|Sidemount Regulator & Hose Routing]]).
2.  **Compatibility with Cylinder Operations**: Deploying DSMBs, clipping cylinders, and switching regulators must be performed while holding the light—practice these skills while wearing your Goodman handle (see [[en/30_Skills/In-Water Cylinder Management Techniques|In-Water Cylinder Management Techniques]]).
3.  **Pre-dive Verification**: Incorporate light status, primary charge, and backup security into your pre-dive checklist (see [[en/70_Resources/Pre-Dive Checklists|Pre-Dive Checklists]]).
