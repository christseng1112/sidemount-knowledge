# Steel vs Aluminum Buoyancy Management (Cylinder Buoyancy & Weighting Management)

In a sidemount diving configuration, the cylinders are suspended along both sides of the diver's body. Therefore, the **material, dry weight, and dynamic buoyancy shifts as gas is consumed** affect the diver's horizontal Trim and buoyancy control more directly than in traditional backmount configurations.

This article contrasts the physical properties and buoyancy curves of **AL80 aluminum cylinders** and **12L steel cylinders**, providing practical ballast management and in-water trim adjustment strategies.

---

## 📊 Physical Principles of Gas Weight & Buoyancy Shift

As we breathe down gas during a dive, the weight of the cylinder in the water decreases because compressed gas itself has mass.
*   For an **11.1-liter (AL80)** cylinder filled to **200 bar**, the compressed air inside has a net weight of approximately **2.7 kg (6 lbs)** [1][2].
*   As the dive progresses and this 2.7 kg of gas is consumed and exhaled, **the cylinder's buoyancy in the water increases by 2.7 kg** [1][2].

| **Total Buoyancy Swing** | 🟢 **Approx. 2.6 kg** (Shifts from sinking to floating) | 🔵 **Approx. 2.7 kg** (Loses weight, but remains negative) |

## ⚖/⚖ Ballast Management Strategies (Weighting Management)

Because these two cylinder types exhibit opposite buoyancy characteristics, divers must apply entirely different weighting and ballast distribution strategies:

### 1. Ballast Strategy for Aluminum Cylinders (AL80): Counteracting Late-Dive Buoyancy
*   **Increase Total Ballast**: To maintain neutral buoyancy during the **5-meter safety stop** at the end of the dive (when each empty cylinder floats with +1.9 kg of positive lift), the diver's **total lead ballast must be increased by approximately 2 to 3 kg (4-6 lbs) compared to diving steel tanks** [5][6].
*   **Ballast Distribution (Gravity Down, Buoyancy Up)**: Because empty aluminum cylinder bottoms float upward strongly at the end of the dive (exerting nearly 2 kg of upward pull per tank), placing weights too high will cause the legs to float. Ballast should be concentrated near the **waist, hips, or crotch strap** (e.g., using a weight belt or the lower slots of a central weight pocket) to keep the lower body down and maintain a horizontal Trim [5][7].

### 2. Ballast Strategy for Steel Cylinders: Weight Reduction & Upper-Body Compensation
*   **Reduce Lead Ballast**: Since steel cylinders remain negatively buoyant (-0.8 kg per tank, or -1.6 kg total) at the end of the dive, they act as active ballast. Divers can **reduce their lead weights by 3 to 5 kg (6-10 lbs)** [5][6][8].
*   **Shift Ballast Upward (Torque Compensation, Preventing Foot-Heavy)**: The weight of steel cylinders is concentrated heavily at the bottom. Tying heavy steel cylinders to the hips often results in a "foot-heavy" posture (legs sinking). To counteract this downward torque, **divers must position their lead weights as high up the torso as possible** (e.g., on the upper shoulder harness, the top slots of a central weight pocket, or the upper end of the **[[en/20_Equipment/Razor Sidemount System Adjustment|Razor 4.0]]** spine pocket) to create a counter-torque and raise the lower body [5][7][9].

## 🏊 In-Water Balancing & Using Sliding D-Rings

The dynamic shift in cylinder buoyancy requires active in-water adjustment of the lower attachment points:

### 🟢 Aluminum Cylinder Operation: Relocating (Clipping Forward)
*   **The Buoyancy Shift**: As cylinder pressure drops to approximately **120 - 100 bar** (representing half of the gas consumed, where buoyancy shifts from negative to +0.6 kg positive), the cylinder bottoms begin to float upward and outward (flaring), breaking the streamlined profile.
*   **The Action**: The diver must unclip the lower bolt snaps from the fixed rear hip D-rings and clip them forward to the **sliding D-rings** on the waist belt. Moving the attachment point forward pulls the floating cylinder bottoms back down toward the body's midline [8][10].
*   **Roll Control**: Unclipping a cylinder temporarily removes the downward weight on that side, causing the diver to roll toward the heavier side. Divers must engage their core and use subtle leg adjustments to maintain a flat horizontal trim during the switch, preventing their body from tilting.

### 🔵 Steel Cylinder Operation: Fixed Position
*   **The Buoyancy Shift**: Because steel cylinders remain negatively buoyant throughout the dive (still -1.5 kg at 50 bar), the cylinder bottoms sink downward and remain flat along the sides without floating up.
*   **The Action**: **Sliding D-rings are not required when diving steel cylinders**. Keep the lower bolt snaps clipped to the fixed rear hip D-rings (or butt plate) throughout the entire dive. However, be aware that the heavy, low-hanging steel bottoms can scrape against wrecks or reefs, requiring good spatial awareness in tight restrictions [5][8].
