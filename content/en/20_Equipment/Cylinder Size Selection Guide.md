# Cylinder Size Selection Guide (Cylinder Size & Type Selection)

This guide focuses on the decision-making process of **selecting the correct cylinders**: volume, material, pressure rating, and symmetrical matching. For the underwater buoyancy shifts and ballast adjustments of these cylinders once selected, see [[en/20_Equipment/Steel vs Aluminum Buoyancy Management|Steel vs Aluminum Buoyancy Management]].

---

## 🎯 The Three Core Decision Factors

1.  **Gas Volume Requirement**: Determined by back-calculating your dive plan—RMV × Time × ATA + Safety Reserve (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Consumption Rate (SAC & RMV) Calculation|Gas Consumption Rate (SAC & RMV) Calculation]] and [[en/40_Gas_Management/Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles|Gas Planning & Safety Reserve Principles]]).
2.  **Exposure Suit Ballast Balance**: Wetsuits are typically paired with aluminum cylinders, whereas drysuits are paired with steel cylinders (see [[en/20_Equipment/Exposure Suit Selection & Trim Influence|Exposure Suit Selection & Trim Influence]]) [1][2].
3.  **Local Supply & Availability**: Traveling divers can easily rent AL80s almost anywhere; specifying a specific cylinder type is usually only practical in your local dive region where you own your gear.

## 📊 Common Sidemount Cylinder Specifications

| Cylinder | Internal Volume | Working Pressure | Available Gas (approx.) | Full Buoyancy | Empty Buoyancy | Typical Use |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **AL80 (S80)** | 11.1 L | 207 bar | ~2,300 L | Slightly Negative | **Positive (approx. +2 kg empty)** | Global standard, cave diving favorite [2][3][4] |
| **AL72** | 10.0 L | 207 bar | ~2,000 L | Slightly Negative | Slightly Positive | Shorter length, excellent Trim, friendly for shorter divers [3] |
| **Steel 12L (232 bar)**| 12.2 L | 232 bar | ~2,800 L | Negative | Slightly Negative | European standard, drysuit cold water [2] |
| **LP85 (Low Pressure Steel)**| ~13 L | 182 bar | ~2,400 L | Negative | Near Neutral | North American favorite: steel volume with near-aluminum buoyancy [3] |
| **Steel 7L / LP50** | 7 L | 232/182 bar | ~1,600 L | Slightly Negative | Near Neutral | Smaller divers, shallow dives, pool training [3] |

> 📋 **Notes & Limitations**: Buoyancy values vary by manufacturer (Luxfer, Catalina, Faber, Worthington) and valve weight; the table indicates **qualitative trends**. For exact specs, check the manufacturer's charts. Available gas is calculated at full working pressure, without deducting reserve gas.

## 🔩 Key Selection Principles

### 1. Left and Right Cylinders Must Be Symmetrical
*   Sidemount gas management is built on **symmetrical gas rotation** between two cylinders (see [[en/30_Skills/Sidemount Regulator Switching Procedure|Sidemount Regulator Switching Procedure]]). Using different cylinder capacities complicates pressure tracking and increases trim imbalance, and should only be done by advanced divers for specialized tasks.
*   Ensure cylinder neck length and valve types (DIN recommended) match, keeping bungee attachment heights symmetrical.

### 2. Aluminum vs. Steel Cylinders
*   **Aluminum Advantages**: The bottom of the cylinder becomes positively buoyant at the end of the dive, which pairs with the "clip-forward technique" to keep the tanks parallel to the body (see [[en/30_Skills/In-Water Cylinder Management Techniques|In-Water Cylinder Management Techniques]]). They are widely available for rent worldwide and are safer for wetsuit diving [1][3].
*   **Steel Advantages**: Larger gas volumes, act as ballast (switching to steel can remove 2–3 kg of lead from your harness), and remain negative throughout the dive [2][3]. **LP85 cylinders represent a hybrid compromise**: steel gas volume with a buoyancy curve close to aluminum [3].

### 3. Pressure Rating & Compressor Realities
*   High-pressure cylinders (300 bar) theoretically carry more gas, but many fill stations can only fill up to 200–232 bar, reducing their real-world volume advantage.
*   Note that 232 bar and 300 bar DIN valves use different thread configurations (5 threads vs. 7 threads), and your first stages must match.

### 4. Considerations for Smaller Divers
*   Double AL80s can be too long for shorter divers, hanging below the knees and interfering with kicking. **AL72, Steel 7L, or LP50** cylinders are suitable alternatives, requiring a more conservative gas plan due to their smaller capacity [3].
