# Cylinder Rigging & Hardware Setup (Sidemount Cylinder Rigging & Hardware)

Cylinder rigging is the most critical hardware skill in sidemount diving. The accuracy of this setup directly determines whether the cylinders sit parallel to the diver's body, whether they swing during swimming (the pendulum effect), and how smoothly the diver can don and doff cylinders in the water.

This article details the core components of the rigging system, the **calculation of the lower bolt snap height, the 45-degree torque alignment method**, the mechanics of neck bungees, and the practical differences between rigging steel and aluminum cylinders.

---

## ⚙ Core Components of the Rigging System

A standard sidemount cylinder rigging kit includes the following hardware:

```
【Cylinder Neck (Neck)】                       【Cylinder Bottom (Butt)】
     [Valve / Handwheel]                          [Cylinder Clamp / Cam Band]
              │                                                │
     [Neck Bungee]                                [Rigging Leash / Cord]
                                                               │
                                                  [Lower Bolt Snap]
```

1.  **Cylinder Clamp (Cylinder Clamp / Cam Band)**:
    *   **Aluminum**: Typically utilizes a stainless steel worm gear hose clamp covered with a protective rubber tube sleeve to prevent cutting the exposure suit or scratching hands [1].
    *   **Steel**: Often utilizes a standard adjustable webbing cam band, which allows for quicker adjustment.
2.  **Lower Bolt Snap (Butt Bolt Snap)**: Typically a **3/8-inch large-eye stainless steel single-ended bolt snap**. The large eye is designed to facilitate smooth clipping and unclipping with a single finger even when wearing thick gloves or when fingers are stiff from cold [1][2].
3.  **Rigging Leash (Rigging Leash / Cord)**: Used to tie the bolt snap tightly to the cylinder clamp. Avoid metal split rings or metal chains; **a cuttable nylon cord must be used** so that in the event of an extreme entanglement emergency, the diver can cut the cylinder free with a line cutter [2][3].

## 📐 Lower Bolt Snap Height Calculation & 45-Degree Torque Alignment

The position of the lower bolt snap is key to controlling the cylinder angle in the water. It requires precise geometric measurement and tying:

### 1. Golden Ratio Height Calculation (Rigging Geometry)
*   **Cylinder Clamp Position**: For the common **AL80 aluminum cylinder**, the stainless steel clamp should be positioned approximately **3 to 4 finger-widths (approx. 8-10 cm)** up from the flat bottom of the cylinder [4][5].
*   **Bolt Snap Hanging Height**: Secure the bolt snap tightly to the clamp using nylon cord. **Once tied, the tail of the bolt snap (sliding gate opening) should align precisely with the flat bottom of the cylinder, or sit no more than 1 cm above it** [4][5][6].
    *   **Leash Too Long (Pendulum Effect)**: If the leash is too long, the cylinder bottom will swing left and right like a pendulum, hitting the diver's thighs and breaking the streamlined profile [6].
    *   **Leash Too Short (Difficult Clipping)**: If the leash is too short, the lack of play makes it extremely difficult to clip the bolt snap to the waist D-ring in the water [6].

```
【Correct Rigging Height】                  【Incorrect Rigging (Too Long)】
       │                                         │
    [Clamp]                                   [Clamp]
       │                                         │
  [Leash (Tight)]                           [Leash (Too Long)] ──> Causes cylinder to drift/swing
       │                                         │
   [Bolt Snap]                                   │
   ┌──┴──┐                                    [Bolt Snap]
   │Cyl  │                                       │
   │Base │                                    ┌──┴──┐
   └─────┘                                    │Cyl  │
                                              │Base │
                                              └─────┘
```

### 2. 45-Degree Torque Alignment Method
When tying the lower bolt snap to the cylinder clamp, its direction relative to the valve handwheel is critical:
*   **Alignment Angle**: Align the valve outlet to point straight forward. The nylon rigging leash attachment point on the clamp must sit **45 degrees offset from the direction of the outlet** [6][7].
*   **Physical Mechanism**: When you clip the lower bolt snap to the waist/hip D-ring in the water, this 45-degree offset creates an inward-rotating torque on the cylinder. This torque forces the cylinder to rotate flat against the diver's side, preventing the cylinder from flaring outward (flaring) and maintaining a streamlined profile [6][7].

## 🔄 Neck Attachment Mechanisms: Bungee Systems Compared

Securing the cylinder neck (valve) relies on elastic bungees attached to the harness shoulder straps. Three main styles are used today:

1.  **Loop Bungee**:
    *   *Mechanism*: Utilized in systems like the **[[en/20_Equipment/Razor Sidemount System Adjustment|Razor 4.0]]**. The bungee forms a fixed loop on the shoulder strap and is wrapped directly around the valve handwheel in the water to secure it.
    *   *Pros*: Pulls the valve tightly into the armpit, offering excellent streamlining, and bungee tension can be easily adjusted using a tri-glide [8][9].
2.  **Continuous Bungee**:
    *   *Mechanism*: A single long bungee cord runs across the diver's back, terminating in bolt snaps at both ends. Before the dive, these snaps are clipped to a small cord loop (choker) on the cylinder neck.
    *   *Pros*: Balanced tension on both sides; the bungee does not snap back behind the diver's back when one cylinder is removed [9].
3.  **Neck Clip (Choker / Neck Clip)**:
    *   *Mechanism*: A webbing strap (choker) is cinched around the cylinder neck, connected to a bolt snap. This bolt snap is clipped directly to the shoulder D-ring, with bungee cord used as a secondary stabilizer.
    *   *Pros*: Provides a robust, metal-on-metal physical connection. Ideal for boat diving in rough seas with high waves, preventing the cylinders from jumping loose on the surface [3][9].

## ⚖/⚖ Rigging Differences: Steel vs. Aluminum Cylinders

Because steel and aluminum cylinders feature opposite weight and buoyancy characteristics in the water, their rigging configurations must be adjusted accordingly:

*   **Aluminum Cylinder (AL80) Rigging**:
    *   *Clamp Material*: A stainless steel worm gear hose clamp must be used because aluminum cylinder walls are smooth, and webbing bands can easily slide.
    *   *Positioning*: Position the clamp higher (approx. 8-10 cm from the bottom) to compensate for the cylinder bottom floating up as it empties [1][10].
    *   *Sliding D-Rings*: Must be paired with **sliding D-rings** so the diver can shift the attachment point forward as the cylinder bottom becomes positive [5][10].
*   **Steel Cylinder Rigging**:
    *   *Clamp Material*: A standard webbing cam band is sufficient.
    *   *Positioning*: Steel cylinders remain negatively buoyant (especially the bottom, which is very heavy). To prevent "foot-heavy" posture, **the steel cylinder clamp should be moved downward (approx. 3-5 cm from the bottom)**. Shifting the clamp down pushes the attachment point toward the bottom of the tank, effectively raising the cylinder relative to the torso and balancing the downward pull [5][10].
    *   *D-Rings*: Steel cylinders do not require sliding D-rings; they remain clipped to the fixed rear hip D-rings throughout the dive [5].
