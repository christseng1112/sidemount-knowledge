# Sidemount Harness & BCD (BCD / Harness & Wing)

In sidemount diving, the **harness** and **buoyancy compensator wing (bladder)** form the structural foundation of the entire configuration and are key to maintaining horizontal Trim. Unlike the jacket-style BCDs common in recreational diving, sidemount harnesses and wings feature a separate or modular design to achieve a highly streamlined profile and maximum range of motion in the water.

This article systematically analyzes the weight-bearing structure of sidemount harnesses, wing design principles, dual-bladder backup systems, and a comparison of the two main design philosophies.

---

## 📐 Harness Systems & Geometric Load-Bearing

The primary purpose of a sidemount harness is to **distribute the heavy weight of the cylinders evenly across the diver's skeleton and securely lock the cylinders in position in the water**.

```
              [ Shoulder Straps ] ──> Support upper cylinder weight, position chest D-rings
                      │
  [ Spine Strap ] ────┼──── [ Waist Strap ] ──> Support weights and lower clips, position hip/sliding D-rings
                      │
              [ Crotch Strap ] ──> Prevents the system from shifting upward toward the head
```

1.  **Shoulder Straps**: Bear the tension of the cylinder neck bungees. The position of the chest D-rings on the shoulder straps is critical for securing cylinder valves and backing regulator clips. Shoulder straps must not be adjusted too tight, or they will limit head rotation and forward arm movement [1].
2.  **Waist Strap**: The foundation of lateral stability. The waist strap must utilize stiff webbing to prevent shifting when heavy cylinders are clipped on. Hip D-rings (or sliding D-rings) placed on the lateral midline of the pelvis serve as lower cylinder attachment points [1][2].
3.  **Crotch Strap**: **The most indispensable anti-slip mechanism in a sidemount system**. When the wing bladder is inflated in the water, the resulting buoyancy pulls the entire harness upward toward the head. The crotch strap anchors the system firmly to the hips, ensuring that the cylinder valves remain locked in the armpits and do not push up against the back of the head [1][3].
4.  **Butt Plate & Butt Rails**: Many heavy-duty American sidemount systems (like Hollis, Dive Rite) feature metal or plastic butt plates with metal rails. This design provides multiple lower attachment points, ideal for mounting three or more stage/decompression cylinders during technical diving [1][4].

## 🎈 Buoyancy Compensator Wing (Wing / Bladder) Design Principles

A sidemount wing must provide sufficient lift while maintaining the lowest possible **cross-sectional area** [5].

### 1. Low Profile & Triangular Shape
Sidemount wings (such as the BAT Wing 4 in the **[[en/20_Equipment/Razor Sidemount System Adjustment|Razor 4.0]]** or the **[[en/20_Equipment/XDEEP Stealth 2.0 Configuration|XDEEP Stealth 2.0]]**) expand flatly outward along the sides of the hips when inflated, keeping a very thin profile along the spine. This design prevents gas from sloshing left and right when the diver rolls [2][5].

### 2. Lift Capacity & Gravity Alignment
*   **Recreational/Aluminum Rigs**: Typically require **20 to 30 lbs (9 to 13 kg)** of lift, featuring an extremely lightweight profile.
*   **Technical/Steel/Multi-Cylinder Rigs**: Require **35 to 45 lbs (16 to 20 kg)** of lift to offset multiple negatively buoyant cylinders [2][6].
*   **Lift Distribution (Mid-to-Lower Back Concentration)**: Sidemount wings are designed to concentrate buoyancy around the **mid-to-lower back and upper hips**. This is because the diver's lungs naturally provide buoyancy for the upper torso, while the cylinder bottoms, weights, and legs are negatively buoyant. Concentrating lift in the lower back perfectly counteracts this sinking force, enabling an effortless horizontal Trim [2][5][7].

## 🚨 Dual Bladder Backup Buoyancy Systems

In technical diving or overhead environments, buoyancy failure (a ruptured BCD that cannot hold air) is life-threatening.

*   **Mechanism**: A dual-bladder wing contains two completely independent air bladders, two sets of dump valves, and two inflator hoses (the primary inflator connects to the right-side first stage, and the backup inflator connects to the left-side first stage) [8].
*   ⚠️ **Note: Some minimalist systems are dual-bladder by default**. For example, the **official specs of the Razor 4 BAT Wing define it as a "triple-layer double redundant" wing, with both the primary and backup bladders providing 45 lbs (approx. 20 kg) of lift** [11]. Thus, the assumption that "minimalist sidemount = single bladder" does not apply to the Razor 4, which has built-in backup buoyancy. Always verify whether a system is a single- or dual-bladder version before purchasing.
*   **Target Audience**: Divers wearing **wetsuits** for technical diving. Because wetsuits compress at depth and offer no backup buoyancy, a single-bladder failure would cause the diver to sink directly to the bottom.
*   **Conditional Exceptions for Drysuit Divers**: Divers wearing a **drysuit** with a light cylinder configuration (e.g., aluminum tanks or drop-weight systems) often use the drysuit as a backup buoyancy chamber, inflating it if the BCD fails. Consequently, they often **do not configure** an extra backup bladder. However, **when carrying heavy steel cylinders or multiple stages, the drysuit squeeze at depth may limit its available lift**, meaning a drysuit should not blindly be assumed to provide sufficient backup. A dual-bladder or alternative redundant lift should still be evaluated [8][9].

## ⚖/⚖ Comparison of Design Philosophies

Sidemount BCDs have evolved into two distinct design schools:

### 🟢 Minimalist Style (Minimalist - represented by Razor 4.0)
*   **Philosophy**: Minimalist, weightless, acting like a "second skin."
*   **Structure**: Uses a continuous webbing harness, a very thin wing positioned close to the back, and no shoulder or waist padding. Weight pockets are integrated directly into the harness spine [2][10].
*   **Pros**: Offers the lowest drag in the water, maximum rotational agility, and is ideal for passing through extremely tight restrictions.
*   **Cons**: Lacks padding on land, making heavy cylinders feel uncomfortable against the collarbone and hips [10].

### 🔵 Modular/Heavy-Duty Style (represented by XDEEP / Hollis)
*   **Philosophy**: Modular design, high abrasion protection, and heavy load capacity.
*   **Structure**: Features independent shoulder and waist webbing, thick shoulder and lumbar padding, and a rugged outer shell made of high-denier ballistic nylon or armored plate fabrics [6][10].
*   **Pros**: Highly comfortable on land, extremely resistant to abrasion during shore diving or wreck penetrations, and facilitates easy staging of multiple cylinders [6][10].
*   **Cons**: Larger frontal profile in the water, heavier dry weight, and slightly higher drag than minimalist systems.
