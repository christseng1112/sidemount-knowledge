# Sidemount Gear Travel Packing & Airline Baggage Guide (Traveling with Sidemount Gear)

Sidemount is one of the **most travel-friendly technical configurations**. It lacks the heavy steel backplate and bulky manifold of backmount doubles; a soft-harness rig like the Razor or Stealth combined with two regulator sets is significantly lighter. However, packing dual regulators, multiple bolt snaps, spools, and cutting tools requires specific packing and airport security strategies [1][2].

---

## 🎒 Carry-On vs. Checked Baggage Rules

**The Golden Rule: If losing it destroys your trip, carry it on** [1].

| Carry-On (Cabin Bag) | Checked Baggage |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Dive Computers** (remove batteries or power off to prevent sensor activation) [2]. | Harness & Wing (lay flat to serve as padding). |
| **Regulators** (two sets, packed in a padded regulator bag) [2]. | Fins (position vertically along suitcase walls) [2]. |
| Mask (in a hard case) + spare prescription mask. | Exposure Suit (place on top as a top cushion) [2]. |
| Certification/Insurance cards, logbook, medication. | **Knives, shears, line cutters** (strictly checked) [1]. |
| | Dive Lights (see battery rules below). |

*   While regulators can be checked (packed securely in the center of your suitcase) [2], sidemount divers carry **two regulator sets—doubling the financial loss and replacement headache**. Carry them in the cabin if weight limits allow.
*   Cabin baggage limits (typically 7–10 kg) can be exceeded easily: two regulator sets weigh approximately 4 kg. Distribute dive computers, masks, and heavy accessories into your jacket pockets or companion bags.

---

## 📦 Packing Layout SOP (The Travel Sandwich) [2]

```
Bottom: Harness & Wing laid flat ──> Sides: Fins upright against suitcase walls
──> Center: Regulators & heavy tools (wrapped in towels) ──> Top: Exposure suit laid flat
──> Gaps: Stuff socks, booties, and soft accessories into open spaces
```

Sidemount packing tips:
*   **Bolt Snaps & Bungees**: These can remain attached to the harness, but **bundle loose snaps together with a Velcro strap or rubber bands**. Loose metal components shifting in a bag often trigger manual security inspections under X-ray.
*   **Spools & Reels**: Wind lines tightly and secure them with rubber bands or tape to prevent them from unraveling and entangling other gear in transit.
*   **Lead Weights**: Never travel with weights. Rent or borrow them at your destination (virtually all dive centers provide weights free of charge or for a nominal fee).

---

## 🔋 Batteries & Hazardous Materials

1.  **Lithium Batteries (Lights, Computers, Cameras)**: Spare lithium batteries and power banks **must be carried in your cabin baggage**. Tape over the battery terminals to prevent short circuits. Batteries installed inside devices can be checked, but removing them for carry-on is safer. Large canister light batteries (>100 Wh) require prior airline approval—verify watt-hour ratings before booking.
2.  **Cylinders**: Divers typically rent cylinders at their destination. If traveling with personal inflation cylinders (e.g., argon or drysuit bottles), you must **remove the valve, leaving the cylinder neck open for inspection**. In most cases, this is not worth the logistical effort.
3.  Gas analyzer oxygen sensors (electrochemical) can be carried in either cabin or checked baggage.

---

## ✈️ Baggage Allowances & Travel Tips

*   Standard economy allowance: 23 kg checked + 7–10 kg carry-on [1]. A complete set of technical sidemount gear (including a 5mm wetsuit) weighs approximately 18–22 kg. **Use a portable luggage scale; weigh your bag when dry, and weigh it again before returning** (wet gear absorbs significant water weight) [2].
*   Certain airlines offer **sports equipment or diving gear allowances** (e.g., Turkish Airlines offers free dive bag allowances on specific routes) [1]. Check the carrier's sporting equipment terms before booking; you often need to register the bag in advance.
*   For regional budget carriers (flights to Okinawa, the Philippines, or Malaysia), purchasing 25–30 kg of baggage allowance in advance is typically cheaper than paying excess fees at the gate.

---

## 🧰 Destination Check-In Sequence

1.  **Inspect Immediately on Arrival**: Open your bags and verify your regulators, dials, and computer screens arrived undamaged. Take photos of any transport damage immediately for insurance claims.
2.  **Assemble and Test**: Assemble your rig at the dive center or pool. Perform a positive pressure test (see [[en/70_Resources/Pre-Dive Checklists|Pre-Dive Checklists]]). Verify your gear maintenance schedule before the trip (see [[en/70_Resources/Sidemount Gear Maintenance & Care Guide|Sidemount Gear Maintenance & Care Guide]]).
3.  **Confirm Logistics**: Re-verify cylinder valve fittings (DIN vs. Yoke, and left-valve availability) with your dive center. For logistics in Taiwan and East Asia, see [[en/70_Resources/Taiwan Sidemount Dive Sites Suitability Guide|Taiwan Sidemount Dive Sites Suitability Guide]].
4.  **Carry a Basic Save-a-Dive Kit**: Pack spare O-rings, bungee cords, zip ties, and hex keys. Remote islands rarely have diving hardware shops.
