# Sidemount Gear Maintenance & Care Guide (Sidemount Gear Maintenance Guide)

Compared to traditional backmount systems, sidemount configurations feature more high-use mechanical accessories, such as **stainless steel bolt snaps, neck bungees, and compact, low-profile wings**. If these components are neglected after contact with saltwater, salt spray, and silt, they are highly prone to issues such as seized metal gates, dry-rotted bungees, or bladder punctures caused by internal salt crystallization.

This article provides standard operating procedures (SOPs) for bolt snap descaling and lubrication, BCD wing interior washing, and harness webbing and bungee wear inspections.

---

## 🔑 1. Bolt Snap Descaling & Lubrication

Bolt snaps are the diver's primary interface for securing cylinders, pockets, and spools. A seized or sticky bolt snap slide gate represents a significant hazard, preventing you from clipping, unclipping, or jettisoning gear in an emergency [1].


*   **Rinsing Technique**:
    *   After every dive day, submerge all bolt snaps in warm fresh water for 10–15 minutes.
    *   **Repeatedly cycle the slide gate** and rotate the swivel eye while submerged to flush out trapped salt crystals and sand from the internal spring chamber [1].
*   **Lubrication Protocol**:
    *   Once dry, apply a minimal amount of **food-grade silicone spray** to the slide track and swivel joint using a cotton swab. Work the mechanism to distribute the lubricant [1].
    *   *Warning*: **Do not use WD-40 or heavy petroleum grease**. WD-40 leaves a sticky residue that attracts dust and sand, while heavy grease degrades harness webbing and traps grit inside the spring chamber [1].
*   **Corrosion Management**: 316 marine-grade stainless steel can still develop minor surface tea-staining. Brush it off gently with a brass or nylon wire brush (do not use steel wool, which can scrape away the protective passivated surface layer). If the gate spring action feels weak or fails to close completely, discard the snap immediately [1].

## 🎈 2. BCD Wing Interior Flushing Protocol (Wing Interior Wash)

During diving, saltwater inevitably enters the bladder through the dump valves or the manual inflator valve. If you only rinse the exterior shell, the saltwater inside will dry and form sharp **salt crystals**. These crystals act like tiny knives, rubbing against and eventually puncturing the delicate inner bladder during inflation and deflation [2][3].

### 🧼 Inner Bladder Rinsing SOP
1.  **Drain Residual Water**: Turn the BCD upside down, depress the manual inflator button (or pull the dump valve cord), and completely drain any trapped saltwater [2][3].
2.  **Add Fresh Water**: Press the manual inflator button and pour approximately **1 to 2 liters of warm fresh water** into the oral inflator mouthpiece [2][3].
3.  **Shake to Rinse**: Release the inflator button to seal the bladder. Shake the BCD vigorously in all directions to ensure the fresh water contacts every fold and inner corner of the bladder to dissolve salt deposits [2][3].
4.  **Drain the Fresh Water**: Invert the BCD and **drain the water through the lower pull dump valve**. This flushes salt residue from the dump valve spring and rubber seal gasket [2][3].
5.  **Repeat**: Repeat the process 2–3 times until the drained water has no salty taste.
6.  **Store Half-Inflated**: **Oral inflate the BCD to approximately 50% capacity and hang it in a cool, well-ventilated area to dry** [2][4].
7.  *Physical Principle*: Hanging a wet bladder completely deflated causes the internal urethane layers to **stick together** as they dry. Inflating it later can tear the inner coating, ruining the bladder [2][4].

## 🛡️ 3. Bungee & Harness Webbing Wear Inspections

Because sidemount rigs operate under high tension and constant contact with overhead structures, regularly inspect all soft materials for signs of degradation [3][5]:

### 1. Bungee Inspections
*   **Degradation Indicators**: Exposure to UV rays, chlorine, and saltwater causes natural rubber bungee cores to dry-rot and crack over time.
*   **Inspection Method**: Before every dive, stretch each bungee firmly. Inspect the outer nylon sheath for snags or fraying, and check if the inner rubber core shows fine white cracks. Replace the bungee immediately if any sign of wear appears [3][5].
*   *Self-Sufficiency Backup*: Keep 2 pre-measured spare bungees with tri-glides stowed in your harness pocket for in-field replacement [5].

### 2. Webbing Wear Point Inspections
Regularly inspect the following high-wear areas [3][5]:
*   **Shoulder Slots**: Where the webbing passes through the shoulder plates.
*   **Waist Hips**: Where the webbing rubs against metal cylinder D-rings and sliding D-rings.
*   **Crotch Strap**: The contact points where the crotch strap runs against exposure suit zippers or harnesses.
*   *Fraying Treatment*: If you see minor fuzz or fraying on the webbing edges, melt it flat with a lighter flame. **If a section of webbing is worn through more than 1/3 of its original thickness, replace it immediately** to prevent in-water failure under cylinder load [3][5].
