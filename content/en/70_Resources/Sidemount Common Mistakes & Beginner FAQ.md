# Sidemount Common Mistakes & Beginner FAQ (Common Mistakes & Beginner FAQ)

This guide summarizes the most common equipment configuration errors, skill mistakes, and general questions encountered by divers transitioning to sidemount. Detailed solutions are linked to their respective topic articles on this site.

---

## ❌ Top 10 Equipment Configuration Mistakes

1.  **Cylinder Valves Too Low or Too Far Forward**: The first stages are not tucked under the armpit, resulting in ceiling impacts and making the valves difficult to reach. $\rightarrow$ Adjust bungee height and the lower cylinder clip position (see [[en/20_Equipment/Cylinder Rigging & Hardware Setup|Cylinder Rigging and Hardware Setup]]) [1].
2.  **Bungees Too Loose**: Cylinder necks hang low and the cylinders flare outward like "wings." $\rightarrow$ Bungee tension should pull the cylinder necks flush against the armpits when in the water (see prevention section in [[en/50_Safety/Bungee Failure & Cylinder Dislodgement Procedures|Bungee Failure & Cylinder Dislodgement Procedures]]) [1][2].
3.  **Harness Webbing Not Customized**: Shoulder straps are too long or waist D-rings are misplaced, making the lower bolt snaps hard to reach. $\rightarrow$ Perform a step-by-step harness fitting (see [[en/20_Equipment/Razor Sidemount System Adjustment|Razor Sidemount System Adjustment]] and [[en/20_Equipment/XDEEP Stealth 2.0 Configuration|XDEEP Stealth 2.0 System Adjustment]]) [2].
4.  **Hoses Too Long or Routed Incorrectly**: The long hose is routed poorly or the backup regulator necklace hangs too low. $\rightarrow$ Follow standard routing guidelines (see [[en/20_Equipment/Sidemount Regulator & Hose Routing|Regulator and LP-HP Hose Routing]] and [[en/30_Skills/Second Stage Stowage, Switching & Retrieval|Second Stage Stowage, Switching & Retrieval]]) [1].
5.  **Copying Backmount Weight Requirements**: Sidemount eliminates the heavy backplate, and cylinder positions alter the center of gravity. $\rightarrow$ Re-evaluate your weight requirements (see [[en/20_Equipment/Exposure Suit Selection & Trim Influence|Exposure Suits Selection & Buoyancy Trim Impact]]).
6.  **Mixing Steel and Aluminum Cylinders**: Steel and aluminum cylinders have different buoyancy curves, causing lateral trim imbalance as gas is consumed. $\rightarrow$ Use identical cylinders (see [[en/20_Equipment/Cylinder Size Selection Guide|Cylinder Selection & Sizing Guide]]).
7.  **Fins Too Heavy**: Diving with heavy rubber fins in warm water with a thin wetsuit drags the legs down, destroying trim. $\rightarrow$ Choose appropriate fins (see [[en/20_Equipment/Fins and Mask Selection|Fins and Mask Selection]]) [3].
8.  **Bolt Snaps Too Small**: Using small clips that are difficult to operate when wearing gloves or under stress. $\rightarrow$ Upgrade to larger-eye bolt snaps.
9.  **Dangling Accessories**: SMBs, spools, and backup lights hanging loosely, creating drag and snag hazards. $\rightarrow$ Consolidate into pocket systems (see [[en/20_Equipment/Accessories and Pocket Configuration|Accessories and Pocket Configuration]]).
10. **Blindly Copying Online Rigs**: Assuming an online setup will work without adjusting it to your body size and cylinder types. $\rightarrow$ Understand the principles and customize parameters to your frame [2].

## ❌ Top 5 Skill Mistakes

1.  **Forgetting to Switch Cylinders**: Breathing from a single cylinder until there is a large pressure imbalance, causing the body to roll to one side. $\rightarrow$ Switch regulators every 20–30 bar (see [[en/40_Gas_Management/Sidemount Gas Switching Procedures|Sidemount Gas Switching Procedures]]) [1].
2.  **Dangling Regulators**: Allowing a regulator to hang loose after a switch. $\rightarrow$ Clip it off or stow it on your necklace immediately (see [[en/30_Skills/Second Stage Stowage, Switching & Retrieval|Second Stage Stowage, Switching & Retrieval]]).
3.  **Using Hands for Propulsion**: Using hand sculls to stabilize, which destroys trim and positioning. $\rightarrow$ Keep hands held in front and rely entirely on fin kicks (see [[en/30_Skills/Sidemount Propulsion Techniques|Sidemount Propulsion Techniques]]).
4.  **Poor Buoyancy Coordination**: Delaying BCD inflation during descent or BCD venting during ascent. $\rightarrow$ Adjust buoyancy in segments (see [[en/30_Skills/Descent Procedure & Hover Control|Sidemount Descent Procedures & Hover Control]] and [[en/50_Safety/Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards|Ascent Rate & Safety Stop Standards]]).
5.  **Neglecting Valve Drills**: Taking more than 10 seconds to locate and shut down a valve during a leak drill. $\rightarrow$ Practice regularly (see [[en/50_Safety/Valve Drills Standard Operating Procedures|Valve Drills Standard Operating Procedures]]).

## ❓ Beginner FAQ

**Q1: Do I need to be a highly experienced diver to learn sidemount?**
No. Most training agencies allow Open Water Divers to enroll in recreational sidemount courses [4]. However, the configuration requires rebuilding muscle memory; approach the course with a beginner's mindset (see [[en/10_Concepts/Suitable Groups & Physiological Considerations|Target Demographics & Physiological Considerations]]).

**Q2: How long is a standard sidemount course?**
A thorough course requires 3–4 days with sufficient in-water time. Two-day courses are typically only long enough to learn how to hang the tanks, not how to swim with them in proper trim [4].

**Q3: Is it necessary to buy my own harness?**
Highly recommended. The primary benefit of sidemount is that the harness is customized to your body. Renting gear and adjusting it every trip defeats the purpose of the configuration [2][4].

**Q4: Can I dive sidemount with a single cylinder?**
Yes. See [[en/30_Skills/Single Cylinder Sidemount Configuration|Single Cylinder Sidemount Configuration]]. This is a useful option when traveling to destinations where double cylinders are not available.

**Q5: Is sidemount safer than backmount?**
Independent gas supplies and direct access to valves are genuine safety advantages (see [[en/10_Concepts/Sidemount vs Backmount Systems|Sidemount vs. Backmount Comparison]]), but safety stems from training and discipline, not the harness style. An untrained sidemount diver is in far more danger than a trained backmount diver.

**Q6: Which harness system should I start with (Razor, Stealth, or others)?**
Take the course before buying gear—a good instructor will let you try different systems. For comparisons, see [[en/70_Resources/Sidemount Certification Systems & Course Comparison|Sidemount Certification Systems & Course Comparison]] and [[en/20_Equipment/Sidemount Harness & BCD|Sidemount Harness & BCD Selection]].
