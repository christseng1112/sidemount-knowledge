# Streamline & In-Water Balance Philosophy (Philosophy of Streamlining & Trim)

The core soul of sidemount diving lies in **streamlining** and **horizontal balance (Trim)**. This is not merely for aesthetics; it is designed to achieve maximum **physical efficiency, energy conservation, and safety** in the water.

This article provides an in-depth analysis of the philosophy of in-water balance in sidemount diving from the perspectives of fluid dynamics and the physics of gravity and buoyancy.

---

## 🌊 Fluid Dynamics & Streamlining

When swimming underwater, a diver must overcome water resistance (drag). According to fluid dynamics, the drag force formula is:

$$F_d = \frac{1}{2} \rho v^2 C_d A$$

Where:
*   \(F_d\) is the drag force.
*   \(\rho\) is the density of the water.
*   \(v\) is the swimming velocity.
*   \(C_d\) is the drag coefficient (determined by body shape and equipment surface roughness).
*   \(A\) is the **cross-sectional area (frontal area)** [1].

### 1. Minimizing Cross-Sectional Area \(A\)
*   **Backmount Disadvantage**: Single or double cylinders mounted on the back protrude upward, increasing the diver's vertical height and significantly expanding the cross-sectional area \(A\) during horizontal swimming.
*   **Sidemount Advantage**: Sidemount positions the cylinders along the sides of the body, tucked within the projection profile of the arms and torso. Viewed from the front, the cylinders are completely "hidden" within the diver's silhouette. This minimizes the cross-sectional area \(A\), dramatically reducing the drag force \(F_d\) [1][2].

### 2. Reducing Gas Consumption Rates (SAC/RMV)
Lower drag means that the diver requires less physical exertion for propulsion. This lowers the heart rate, which significantly reduces the Surface Air Consumption (SAC) rate. In long-distance technical diving, this translates to conserving more gas to handle emergencies [2][3].

## ⚖/⚖ Physics of Gravity & Buoyancy Balance (CoG vs CoB)

To maintain a perfect horizontal Trim in the water, the **Center of Gravity (CoG)** and the **Center of Buoyancy (CoB)** must align vertically. Any horizontal misalignment between the two creates a rotational torque, forcing the diver's head or feet to sink [4][5].

$$\tau = F_g \times d$$

Where \(\tau\) is the torque, and \(d\) is the horizontal distance between the Center of Gravity and the Center of Buoyancy.

```
【Imbalanced State (Torque Generated)】
          Buoyancy (CoB) ▲
                         │
    ◄──────── d ────────►│
    │
    ▼ Gravity (CoG) (Causes feet to sink)

【Balanced State (Zero Torque)】
               ▲ Buoyancy (CoB)
               │
               ● (CoG and CoB vertically aligned)
               │
               ▼ Gravity (CoG) (Perfect Horizontal Trim)
```

### The "Golden Triangle" of Sidemount Trim
1.  **Flexibility in Weight Distribution**: Sidemount harness designs typically place ballast along the spine (such as XDEEP's central weight pocket system) or on the waist webbing. This allows divers to micro-adjust weight positioning vertically, directly shifting the CoG [4][6].
2.  **Buoyancy Compensation**: Sidemount wings (bladders) are designed as flat, triangular shapes with volume concentrated around the hips and lower back. This matches and compensates for the negative buoyancy of the lower body (bones and legs), aligning the CoB with the CoG to achieve a natural, effortless horizontal float [4][7].
3.  **Harness Geometry**: To maintain Trim, the diver's **shoulders, hips, and knees** should remain in a single horizontal plane, with knees bent 90 degrees and ankles flexed. Since sidemount cylinders hang parallel along this axis, they do not apply lateral twisting torques to the spine [4][5].

## 🔄 Dynamic Cylinder Trim

One of the greatest technical challenges in sidemount diving is that **cylinder buoyancy shifts dynamically as gas is consumed**. This buoyancy swing is especially pronounced with commonly used **aluminum cylinders (AL80)** [8].

| Cylinder State | Pressure (bar) | Buoyancy in Water (AL80 Aluminum) | Impact on Trim & Balance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Full** | 200 bar | Approx. **-0.7 kg** (Negatively buoyant / Sinks) | Cylinders sit flat along the sides, stable center of gravity. |
| **Half** | 100 bar | Approx. **+0.6 kg** (Slightly positively buoyant) | Cylinder bottoms begin to float slightly. |
| **Empty** | ~0 bar (near reserve) | Approx. **+1.9 kg** (Highly positively buoyant / Floats) | Cylinder bottoms float upward, breaking horizontal Trim [8]. |

### 🛠/🛠 Mitigation Strategy: Physics of Sliding D-Rings
To keep cylinders parallel to the body and maintain a streamlined profile throughout the dive, the lower attachment points must be adjusted dynamically [9][10].

*   **Start of the Dive**: Cylinders are heavy and negatively buoyant. The bottle snaps are clipped to fixed D-rings at the rear of the hips (or rear sliding D-ring positions).
*   **End of the Dive**: Once cylinder pressure drops below approximately 120 bar and the bottoms float upward, the diver slides the lower bolt snaps forward toward the lower abdomen, clipping them to **sliding D-rings**.
*   **Physical Effect**: Shifting the attachment point forward pulls the floating cylinder bottoms back down toward the body's midline, ensuring that the cylinders remain perfectly parallel to the water flow whether full or empty [9][10][11].
