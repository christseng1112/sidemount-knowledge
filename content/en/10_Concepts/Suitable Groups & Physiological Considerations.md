# Suitable Groups & Physiological Considerations (Who Should Dive Sidemount?)

Sidemount is not necessarily a "must-learn" for everyone. However, it is a **highly adjustable and physically friendly** equipment configuration that resolves many physiological and logistical challenges that backmount systems cannot [1][2]. This article outlines the groups that benefit most (and least) from sidemount, as well as the physiological self-assessments required before transitioning.

---

## ✅ Groups That Benefit Most

### 1. Divers with Back, Shoulder, or Joint Injuries
*   On land, backmount doubles force the diver to **lean forward to counter the backward torque**, putting significant pressure on the lower spine. In contrast, sidemount cylinders distribute their weight vertically along the sides of the body, **aligning with the spine's natural load-bearing axis**, allowing the diver to walk upright [1].
*   Since sidemount cylinders are independent, they can be **carried individually** to the water, or even **donned in the water** (where they are nearly weightless), significantly reducing load-bearing time on land [1][2].

### 2. Older Divers and Divers with Smaller Frames
*   Carrying a single AL80 (approx. 14 kg) is far more manageable than hauling a backmount twinset (which can exceed 45 kg).
*   Sidemount harnesses can be custom-fitted to the diver's torso, making them more snug and stable for smaller-framed divers compared to standard-sized metal backplates [2][3].

### 3. Solo or Self-Reliant Divers Seeking Gas Redundancy
*   Sidemount provides two **completely independent** gas delivery systems: if one first stage, second stage, or valve fails, the other cylinder remains intact, providing twice the redundancy and self-rescue capability of a single tank [2]. See [[en/10_Concepts/Self-Sufficiency Philosophy|Self-Sufficiency Philosophy]].

### 4. Cave, Wreck, and Underwater Photographers
*   The flat profile and lack of valves behind the head **minimize vertical profile**, making it ideal for low bedding planes. Cylinders can be unclipped and pushed forward to pass through tight restrictions (see [[en/60_Advanced/Solo Diving & Extreme No-Mount Rigging|Solo & No-Mount Rigging]]) [2].
*   Photographers benefit from stable horizontal Trim and clear space in front of their chest to handle camera rigs [2].

### 5. Travel Divers
*   Sidemount harnesses (e.g., Razor, Stealth) are highly packable and lightweight (approx. 2–4 kg), making them much easier to pack than backmount double systems (backplate, STA, twin tank bands) [2]. Divers can simply rent two single tanks at their destination.

## ⚠️ Pre-Transition Physiological Self-Assessment

| Assessment Metric | Requirement | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Shoulder Mobility** | Ability to reach under the armpits and touch the cylinder valves. | Essential prerequisite for valve drills and emergency shut-offs (see [[en/50_Safety/Valve Drills Standard Steps|Valve Drills Standard Steps]]) [4]. |
| **Core Stability** | Ability to maintain horizontal Trim while micro-adjusting the center of gravity. | Since cylinders sit on the sides, the center of gravity shifts as gas is consumed, requiring core compensation. |
| **Single-Side Lifting** | Ability to lift a single cylinder (approx. 14–20 kg) with one hand. | Cylinders must still be carried individually on land/boats. |
| **Willingness to Unlearn** | Commitment to rebuilding foundational habits. | Mid-water buoyancy, propulsion kicks, and gas rotation require rebuilding muscle memory [3]. |

> 📌 **Insufficient shoulder mobility $\neq$ disqualification from sidemount**. Most issues with "not being able to reach the valves" are actually caused by poor harness adjustments (incorrect shoulder strap length or bungee position) rather than physical limitations [4].

---

## ❌ Scenarios Where Sidemount is Less Suitable

1.  **"Grab and Go" Resort Divers**: Customizing sidemount rigging (harness adjustment, hose routing, bungee height) takes time, and changing cylinder types (steel vs. aluminum) requires re-configuring the bungees and clips [2][3].
2.  **Large Liveaboards with Fixed Workflows**: Some liveaboards are configured for backmount entry and exit. Handing down double cylinders and donning them in the water can slow down the deck team's rotation [2].
3.  **Untrained Divers**: Sidemount is not simply "clipping tanks to your sides." Gas management (rotating cylinders to balance gas) and handling buoyancy shifts require formal training; do not attempt to self-teach [3][4].
